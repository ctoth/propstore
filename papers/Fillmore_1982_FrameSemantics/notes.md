# Frame Semantics

## One-Sentence Summary
Fillmore introduces frame semantics as a research program where understanding a word's meaning requires understanding the structured background knowledge (frame) that the word presupposes, providing the foundational theory for FrameNet and structured lexical semantics.

## Problem Addressed
Formal (truth-conditional) semantics fails to capture how word meanings depend on background knowledge structures, experiential context, and institutional presuppositions. Fillmore argues that a purely denotational approach cannot explain why words like BACHELOR, WEEKEND, STINGY/THRIFTY, or BUY/SELL carry the semantic baggage they do, and proposes frame semantics as the empirical alternative. *(p.000-R)*

## Key Contributions
- Defines "frame" as a system of categories whose interrelationships are structured by a motivating context, such that understanding any one category requires understanding the whole structure *(p.005-L)*
- Distinguishes between a word *invoking* a frame (activating the entire background structure) and *evoking* specific elements within that frame *(p.006-L)*
- Demonstrates that the same real-world situation can be described by different words that impose different frame perspectives (e.g., LAND vs GROUND, COAST vs SHORE, STINGY vs THRIFTY) *(p.006)*
- Shows that prototype effects in categorization arise from frame structure (BACHELOR presupposes a marriage institution frame) *(p.004-005)*
- Introduces the commercial transaction frame (BUY/SELL/PAY/SPEND/COST/CHARGE) as a paradigm case where multiple verbs access the same underlying frame from different perspectives *(p.003)*
- Argues that understanding word meaning is inseparable from understanding background experiences and institutions *(p.012-013)*
- Traces the historical development from case grammar through generative semantics to frame semantics *(p.001-003)*

## Methodology
This is a theoretical/conceptual paper. Fillmore proceeds by:
1. Reviewing the historical development of the frame concept from case grammar
2. Presenting detailed lexical examples that resist treatment by formal semantics alone
3. Building the frame semantics framework through progressive elaboration of examples
4. Demonstrating applications to semantic change, legal language, text semantics, evaluative language, and presupposition

No formal equations or computational models are presented. The contribution is conceptual/theoretical.

## Key Definitions and Concepts

### Frame (Definition)
A **frame** is a system of categories structured in accordance with some motivating context, such that to understand any one of them you have to understand the whole structure in which it fits. When one of the things in such a structure is introduced into a text or conversation, all of the others are automatically made available. *(p.000-R, p.005-L)*

### Invoking vs Evoking
- **Invoking** a frame: a word or expression activates the entire background frame structure *(p.006-L)*
- **Evoking** a frame: the word picks out specific elements or perspectives within the invoked frame *(p.006-L)*

### Prototype
The concept of "prototype" refers to category membership organized around best examples rather than necessary and sufficient conditions. Frame semantics explains prototype effects: BACHELOR is a prototypical example because it presupposes a particular social institution (marriage) and applies most naturally to participants in that institution who meet certain expectations. The Pope, Tarzan, and a man in a long-term unmarried partnership are all technically unmarried adult males but sit uncomfortably in the BACHELOR category because they violate frame presuppositions. *(p.004-005)*

### Case Frame (Historical)
Fillmore's earlier "case frame" concept: a characterization of a small abstract scene or situation, with the verb as an element that has its own properties but also identifies participants and roles within the scene. E.g., a verb of motion involves a thing that moves, a place it starts from, a place it ends at, a path, etc. The word's meaning selects the case frame perspectives. *(p.001-002)*

### Commercial Transaction Frame
The paradigm example of frame semantics. The words BUY, SELL, PAY, SPEND, COST, and CHARGE all access the same underlying frame involving a buyer, a seller, goods, and money. Each verb profiles different elements of this frame:
- BUY focuses on the actions of the Buyer with respect to the Goods, backgrounding the Seller and Money *(p.003-L)*
- SELL focuses on the actions of the Seller with respect to the Goods and Money *(p.003-L)*
- PAY focuses on the Buyer's actions with respect to Money *(p.003-L)*
- SPEND, COST, CHARGE each provide yet another perspective *(p.003-L)*

This demonstrates that word meaning cannot be captured by denotation alone — the frame provides the structured background against which the word's contribution is defined. *(p.003)*

### Background vs Foregrounded Meaning
A word simultaneously:
1. Invokes a background frame (the structured knowledge prerequisite)
2. Foregrounds (profiles) specific elements of that frame
3. The backgrounded elements are presupposed, not asserted

Example: "chase" presupposes that the chaser and the chasee are both moving in the same direction, with the chaser behind; what it asserts (profiles) is the chaser's intention to catch the chasee. *(p.012-013)*

## Methods & Implementation Details

### Alternate Framings of the Same Situation *(p.006-007)*
The same objective situation can be framed differently by different words:
- LAND vs GROUND: both refer to dry solid surface of the earth, but LAND contrasts with SEA (viewed from water perspective) while GROUND contrasts with AIR (viewed from above) *(p.006)*
- COAST vs SHORE: COAST imposes land perspective (coast of France), SHORE imposes water perspective (shore of the lake) *(p.006)*
- STINGY vs THRIFTY: same behavior (not spending money), but STINGY evaluates negatively from the perspective of those affected, THRIFTY evaluates positively from the perspective of the person's financial prudence *(p.006-007)*

### Frame-Internal vs Frame-External Knowledge *(p.003)*
Early in the paper's history, Fillmore attempted to distinguish:
- Frame-internal semantics: knowledge needed to define the roles/structure within the frame
- Frame-external knowledge: real-world knowledge about how the frame connects to broader experience

He concluded this distinction could not be sharply maintained — understanding a word requires both. *(p.003)*

### Evaluative Frames *(p.010)*
Evaluative adjectives like GOOD, EFFICIENT, INTELLIGENT, GRANT are frame-dependent:
- "Good coffee" uses gustatory evaluation frame
- "Good mother" uses nurturing/responsibility evaluation frame  
- REAL operates against the background of counterfeit possibility — "real coffee" implies distinguishing from substitutes *(p.011-012)*

### Legal Language and Frame Semantics *(p.008-009)*
Legal terms provide examples of technical frame-dependence:
- MURDER requires: unlawful killing + premeditated intent (frame elements) *(p.009-L)*
- FORCIBLE ENTRY legal definition differs from common understanding — legal usage may include cases without physical force *(p.009)*
- INNOCENT and GUILTY presuppose different frames in legal vs conversational usage *(p.009)*

### Semantic Change Through Frame Change *(p.008)*
Two types of semantic change:
1. **Background frame changes** while word usage stays constant: e.g., NIECE/NEPHEW originally restricted to specific lineage, now generalized as kinship systems simplified *(p.008)*
2. **Word usage changes** (metaphorical extension, narrowing, broadening) while underlying frame remains: e.g., a word's range expands or contracts within its frame *(p.008)*

### Text-Level Framing *(p.009-010)*
- Different text genres impose different framing conventions
- Robert Longacre's work on text structure shows languages develop different discourse-framing strategies
- The choice of frame at text level guides interpretation of individual sentences *(p.010)*

### Paradigmatic Structure and Taxonomies *(p.010-011)*
- Words often belong to paradigmatic sets: MAN/WOMAN, BOY/GIRL form a 2x2 paradigm (gender x maturity)
- BACHELOR/SPINSTER/HUSBAND/WIFE form another paradigm within marriage institution frame
- VERTEBRATE and MAMMAL demonstrate taxonomic frame membership *(p.011)*
- CAT vs KITTY: different "basic level" status per Rosch (1975) — CAT is basic level, KITTEN/KITTY is not *(p.011-012)*

### Presupposition vs Assertion *(p.012-013)*
Frame semantics provides a principled account of the presupposition/assertion distinction:
- A word's frame elements are presupposed (backgrounded)
- What the word contributes beyond the frame is asserted (foregrounded)
- "Chase" presupposes motion in same direction; asserts pursuit intention *(p.012-013)*
- This matters for understanding what is "at issue" in a given usage

## Results Summary
Fillmore demonstrates through extensive lexical analysis that word meanings cannot be understood in isolation from structured background knowledge (frames). The paper establishes frame semantics as a viable alternative to truth-conditional formal semantics for understanding how humans comprehend and produce language. Key results include: (1) the commercial transaction frame as proof that multiple words can access the same underlying knowledge structure from different perspectives, (2) prototype effects explained through frame presuppositions (BACHELOR), (3) evaluative adjectives shown to be frame-dependent, (4) a principled account of the presupposition/assertion distinction through frame foregrounding. *(p.000-013)*

## Limitations
- The paper is explicitly non-formal: Fillmore states "I think I would be faking it, and as well as I can I wish to describe linguistic phenomena which must be well understood and carefully described before serious formal theorizing about them can become possible" *(p.000-R)*
- No computational implementation is proposed
- The boundary between frame-internal and frame-external knowledge is acknowledged as difficult to maintain *(p.003)*
- No systematic method for enumerating or discovering frames is provided — examples are hand-selected
- The relationship between frame semantics and formal logic is left unexplored
- Cross-linguistic validity is gestured at but not systematically investigated

## Arguments Against Prior Work
- **Against formal/truth-conditional semantics:** Cannot capture the background knowledge needed to understand word meaning; misses the perspective-imposing nature of lexical choice *(p.000-R)*
- **Against purely distributional approaches:** Words that are distributional substitutes (STINGY/THRIFTY) carry entirely different frame perspectives, which distributional methods alone cannot capture *(p.006-007)*
- **Against checklist/feature semantics:** The BACHELOR example shows that defining category membership via necessary and sufficient features fails — frame presuppositions explain why edge cases (Pope, Tarzan) feel wrong even when features match *(p.004-005)*
- **Against treating word meaning as purely referential:** Words don't just denote — they impose a particular perspective on reality via frame selection (LAND vs GROUND, COAST vs SHORE) *(p.006)*

## Design Rationale
- **Why "frame" rather than "schema" or "script"?** Fillmore uses "frame" as a general cover term for the set of concepts variously called schema, script, cognitive model, folk theory, etc. He prefers it for its generality and its connection to Minsky's (1975) AI work. *(p.000-R)*
- **Why empirical over formal?** Fillmore explicitly positions frame semantics as closer to ethnographic semantics than formal semantics — the goal is first to describe and understand phenomena, not to axiomatize them *(p.000-R)*
- **Why organize around motivating contexts?** Because the meaning of a word is not independent of the structured situation type it presupposes — you cannot understand WEEKEND without the calendar frame, or BACHELOR without the marriage institution frame *(p.004-005)*

## Testable Properties
- A word that invokes a frame should activate (make available for reference) all elements of that frame, even those not directly named by the word *(p.000-R)*
- Words that access the same frame from different perspectives (BUY/SELL) should share frame elements but differ in which elements are foregrounded/backgrounded *(p.003)*
- Prototype judgments for a category should correlate with fit to the presupposed frame, not just feature matching (BACHELOR test: unmarried males who violate frame presuppositions should be judged as worse category members) *(p.004-005)*
- Evaluative adjectives (GOOD, REAL) should show different interpretations depending on the frame of the modified noun *(p.010)*
- When a word's background frame changes historically, the word's usage should shift even if its "definition" stays constant *(p.008)*

## Relevance to Project
**Rating: High** — This is the foundational theory paper for propstore's concept layer. It provides the theoretical justification for why propstore needs a concept registry at all, why concepts are not just labels, and why the non-commitment discipline is semantically necessary.

Fillmore's central thesis — that understanding a word requires understanding the entire structured background frame it presupposes (p.005-L) — is the theoretical foundation for propstore's concept registry. A concept in propstore is not a label; it is a frame: a structured system of related categories, dimensions, and presuppositions. Registering a concept means making that frame structure explicit.

Specific points of contact:
1. **Concepts are frames, not labels**: Fillmore's definition of a frame — "a system of categories structured in accordance with some motivating context, such that to understand any one of them you have to understand the whole structure" (p.005-L) — is the theoretical definition of what a propstore concept *is*. The `definition` field in `knowledge/concepts/*.yaml` should capture frame structure, not just a gloss.
2. **Competing framings = non-commitment discipline**: The paper's core demonstration — that LAND vs GROUND, COAST vs SHORE, STINGY vs THRIFTY describe the same reality from different frame perspectives (p.006) — is propstore's non-commitment discipline stated as a linguistic principle. Multiple rival normalizations are not an engineering compromise; they are semantically necessary because different frames are genuinely different ways of understanding the same phenomenon.
3. **Vocabulary reconciliation requires frame analysis**: The commercial transaction frame (BUY/SELL/PAY/SPEND/COST/CHARGE all accessing the same frame from different perspectives, p.003) shows that vocabulary reconciliation cannot be purely lexical. Two terms may refer to the same underlying frame element but foreground different aspects — reconciliation must operate at the frame level, not the string level.
4. **Core/peripheral distinction for concept dimensions**: Fillmore's distinction between what a word asserts (foregrounds) vs presupposes (backgrounds) (p.012-013) maps to the distinction between a concept's core dimensions (what claims about it must specify) and its presupposed frame structure (what is taken for granted).
5. **Frame change as concept evolution**: Fillmore's two types of semantic change — background frame changes while usage stays constant, or usage changes while frame remains (p.008) — provide the theoretical model for how propstore should handle concept evolution over time without destroying history.
