# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

Scientific publications are documentary representations of defeasible arguments, supported by data and reproducible methods. They are the essential mediating artifacts in the ecosystem of scientific communications, the institutional "goal" of science is publishing results. The linear document publication format, dating from 1665, has survived transition to the Web.

Interactable publication volume; the difficulty of verifying evidence; and observed problems in evidence and citation distortions suggest a need for a very flexible yet machine-readable model of scientific publications. This model should support digital communications, evidence examination, challenge, verification and reuse, and incremental adoption. Such a model must be capable of expressing a broad spectrum of representational complexity, ranging from minimal to maximal formats.

Results

The Micropublications semantic model of scientific argument and evidence provides these features. Micropublications support natural language statements, data, methods, and materials specifications; discussion and commentary; challenge and disagreement, as well as allowing many kinds of statement formalization.

The minimal form of a micropublication is a statement with its attribution. The maximal form is a statement with its complete supporting argument, consisting of all relevant evidence, interpretation, discussion and challenges brought forward in support of or opposition to it. The spectrum of forms between these two endpoints supports nine significant use cases in nine major activity complexes we identified for the biomedical communications ecosystem.

Micropublications may be connected into deep multilateral citation / evidence networks across large corpora. They have the advantage of a transparent internal structure opening their foundational evidence to view. These sub-work-themselves may be published as micropublications. Micropublications may be formalized and serialized in multiple ways, including in RDF. They may be added to publications as stand-off metadata.

An OWL 2 vocabulary for micropublications is available at http://purl.org.

Conclusion

We suggest that micropublications, generated by useful software tools supporting such activities as writing, editing, reviewing, and discussion, will be of great value in improving the quality and traceability of biomedical communications.

Micropublications, because they model evidence and allow qualified, nuanced assertions, can play essential roles in the scientific communications ecosystem in places where simpler, formalized and purely statement-based models, such as the nanopublication model, will not be sufficient. At the same time they will add significant value to, and are intentionally compatible with, statement-based formalizations.

Keywords

argumentation, annotation, data citation, digital abstract, scientific discourse, scientific evidence, methods citation, research reproducibility, nanopublications, web of science

## Our Interpretation

The paper addresses the fundamental problem that scientific publications are argumentative structures but existing machine-readable models (SWAN, nanopublications, BEL) cannot capture their full argumentative depth — particularly transitive evidence chains, multipolar support/challenge, and linkage to empirical data. The Micropublications model fills this gap with a formally defined framework grounded in argumentation theory that can represent claims at varying levels of formalization. This is directly relevant to propstore as it provides the conceptual foundation for claim representation with provenance, bipolar argumentation structure, and vocabulary normalization through Similarity Groups.
