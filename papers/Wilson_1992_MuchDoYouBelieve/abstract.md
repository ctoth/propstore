# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

This paper responds to a number of criticisms of Dempster-Shafer theory made by Judea Pearl. He criticises Dempster-Shafer belief for not obeying the laws of Bayesian belief; however, these laws lead to well-known problems in the face of ignorance, and seem unreasonably restrictive. It is argued that it is not reasonable to expect a measure of belief to obey Pearl's sandwich principle. The standard representation of "if-then" rules in Dempster-Shafer theory, criticised by Pearl, is justified and favorably contrasted with a conditional probability representation.

KEYWORDS: Dempster-Shafer theory, Bayesian probability, lower probability, conditioning, the sandwich principle, if-then rules

---

## Our Interpretation

Wilson's reply to Pearl 1990 argues that Bayesian probability cannot adequately represent ignorance because of arbitrary frame-dependence under indifference, that Pearl's sandwich principle Belf(a|b) ≤ Belf(a) ≤ Belf(a|¬b) is violated by any honest measure of evidential support, and that Dempster's rule of conditioning encodes a sensible "irrelevance" default that Bayesian/FH conditioning lacks. Wilson interprets D-S simple-support rules `(n ∧ a) → b` with `Pr(n)=α` as hidden-condition material implications, recovering contraposition, chaining, and reasoning-by-cases under standard independence assumptions; he then identifies five concrete problems with Pearl's conditional-probability reading of defeasible rules. The Philippe-Pearl-Mary 1000-disease counterexample, where Bayesian + symmetry + sandwich force an absurd P(a₁|b₁)=0.001 while D-S gives 0.495, is the rhetorical centerpiece.
