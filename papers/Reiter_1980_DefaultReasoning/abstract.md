# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

The need to make default assumptions is frequently encountered in reasoning about incompletely specified worlds. Inferences sanctioned by default are best viewed as beliefs which may well be modified or rejected by subsequent observations. It is this property which leads to the non-monotonicity of any logic of defaults.

In this paper we propose a logic for default reasoning. We then specialize our treatment to a very large class of commonly occurring defaults. For this class we develop a complete proof theory and show how to interface it with a top down resolution theorem prover. Finally, we provide criteria under which the revision of derived beliefs must be effected.

---

## Our Interpretation

The paper formalizes default reasoning --- drawing conclusions "in the absence of information to the contrary" --- as a non-monotonic extension of first-order logic. The key finding is that for "normal defaults" (the most common form), a complete proof theory exists that interfaces cleanly with resolution theorem provers, though the general extension membership problem is not semi-decidable. This is directly relevant as the theoretical foundation for the truth maintenance and assumption-based reasoning systems already in the propstore collection.
