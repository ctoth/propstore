# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

An abstract framework for structured arguments is presented that instantiates Dung's (1995) abstract argumentation frameworks. Arguments are defined as inference trees formed by applying two kinds of inference rules: strict rules and defeasible rules. This leads to three ways of attacking an argument: attacking a premise, attacking a conclusion and attacking an inference. To resolve such conflicts, preferences may be used, which leads to three corresponding kinds of defeat: undermining, rebutting and undercutting defeat. The resulting framework integrates work of Pollock, Vreeswijk and others on the structure of arguments in argumentation with Dung's theory of abstract argumentation, in such a way that Dung's theory of abstract argumentation, in such a way that Dung's theory of abstract argumentation is applied to arguments with structure. Various existing approaches are proven to be a special case of the framework, including assumption-based argumentation.

---

## Our Interpretation

This paper bridges the gap between Dung's purely abstract argumentation (where arguments and attacks are atomic) and practical argumentation systems that need to reason with rules and premises. It unifies three independently developed attack types (undermining, rebutting, undercutting) into a single framework and proves that key rationality properties (consistency, closure) hold under well-defined conditions. The framework serves as the formal foundation for ASPIC+, which is one of the most widely-used structured argumentation frameworks in the field.
