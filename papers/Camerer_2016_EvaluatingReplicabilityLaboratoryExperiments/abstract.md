# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

The replicability of some scientific findings has recently been called into question. To contribute data about replicability in economics, we replicate 18 studies published in the American Economic Review and the Quarterly Journal of Economics between 2011 and 2014. All of these replications follow predefined analysis plans that were made publicly available beforehand, and all have a statistical power of at least 90% to detect the original effect size. We find a significant effect in the same direction as in the original study for 11 replications (61%); on average, the replicated effect size is 66% of the original. The replicability rate varies between 67% and 78% for four additional replicability indicators for which we find significant effects. Combining original and replication data, we find significant effects for 15 of the 18 studies. Prediction markets set up by the authors in advance of the replications suggest that weights on the prior probabilities of replication and publication bias can partly explain why some studies replicate and others do not.

---

## Our Interpretation

This study addresses the replication crisis in economics by systematically replicating 18 high-profile lab experiments with high statistical power (90%+). The key finding is that economics lab experiments replicate at a higher rate (61%) than comparable psychology experiments (36% in RPP), but still show substantial effect size shrinkage (mean 66% of originals). The prediction market and survey components demonstrate that expert beliefs can partially anticipate replication outcomes, providing empirical grounding for systems that need to assign reliability weights to scientific claims.
