This paper characterizes strong equivalence for Dung's abstract argumentation frameworks across all major semantics (stable, preferred, complete, admissible, grounded, semi-stable, ideal, eager). Two AFs are strongly equivalent when they can be freely substituted in any larger context without changing the extensions. The central technical contribution is the identification of kernel operators -- syntactic transformations that remove "redundant" attacks from self-attacking arguments -- such that two AFs are strongly equivalent if and only if their kernels are identical. Different kernel operators are needed for different semantic families: the a-kernel works for stable/preferred/semi-stable/ideal/eager, the a*-kernel for admissible/complete, and a separate characterization handles grounded semantics. The paper also studies weaker notions: equivalence in terms of skeptical/credulous consequences (p-equivalence) and local equivalence (restricting the added context to share the same argument set). Self-loop free AFs have trivial strong equivalence (syntactic identity), making self-loops the sole source of non-trivial equivalence phenomena.
