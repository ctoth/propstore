## Aarts_2015_EstimatingReproducibilityPsychologicalScience  (reproducibility, meta-science, replication, effect-sizes, base-rates)
Presents the first large-scale systematic replication of 100 psychological studies from three major journals, finding that only 36% of replications achieved statistical significance and replication effect sizes averaged roughly half the originals. Defines the AGM postulates for rational belief contraction and revision, and constructs explicit partial meet contraction and revision functions via selection over remainder sets (maximal consistent subsets). Defines Local Models Semantics (LMS), a formal semantics for contextual reasoning built on two principles: locality (reasoning uses only part of what is available, constituting a "context") and compatibility (reasoning across contexts must be mutually consistent). Presents a framework for nonmonotonic reasoning based on preferred maximal consistent subsets (preferred subtheories), where defaults are distinguished from facts not by logical form but by priority when conflicts arise. They introduce CF2 semantics, which uses maximal conflict-free sets as the base function and correctly handles odd-length attack cycles and self-defeating arguments — cases where preferred semantics produces only the empty extension.

## Camerer_2016_EvaluatingReplicabilityLaboratoryExperiments  (replication, experimental-economics, prediction-markets, meta-science)
Camerer et al. systematically replicate 18 laboratory economics experiments from AER and QJE (2011-2014), finding that 11/18 (61%) replicate at p<0.05 with mean effect sizes at 66% of originals. Prediction markets and expert surveys among economists provide moderate forecasting accuracy for which studies will replicate. The study provides empirical base rates for replication success in experimental economics, offering calibration data for systems that reason about claim reliability based on study characteristics like original p-value and sample size.

## Camerer_2018_EvaluatingReplicabilitySocialScience  (replication, meta-science, prediction-markets, effect-sizes, social-science)
Camerer et al. systematically replicate 21 social science experiments published in Nature and Science between 2010 and 2015, finding that 13/21 (62%) replicate with effect sizes averaging 50% of originals. Prediction markets by academic peers strongly predict which studies replicate (Spearman rho = 0.842), and a Bayesian mixture model estimates the true positive rate of published findings at 67% with a replication deflation factor of 0.71. This paper provides empirical base rates essential for calibrating uncertainty in any system that reasons about published scientific claims, directly informing opinion strength priors and the non-commitment discipline of holding multiple rival assessments of the same finding.

## Errington_2021_InvestigatingReplicabilityPreclinicalCancer  (replicability, cancer-biology, meta-analysis, effect-size, reproducibility)
Reports the results of the Reproducibility Project: Cancer Biology, which systematically replicated 50 experiments from 23 high-impact preclinical cancer biology papers, finding replication effect sizes approximately 85% smaller than originals and replication rates ranging from 3% to 82% depending on criterion used.
Provides empirical base rates for replicability across seven distinct criteria, moderator analysis of five candidate predictors (none significant), and combined meta-analytic estimates showing that most original positive findings retain directional support but with substantially deflated magnitudes.
Relevant to calibrating uncertainty in argumentation systems: the multi-criterion replication rate spectrum and systematic effect size shrinkage provide concrete priors for adjusting claim confidence based on replication status.
