The chapter is a systematic taxonomy: for every framework type the author defines a
*normal form*, proves a semantics-preservation theorem, packages the normal form as
a numbered *Translation*, redefines the theorem in translation-system vocabulary,
classifies the translation along a fixed property checklist (full/target-subclass,
overlapping, argument-domain-preserving, generic, semantics-domain-preserving,
exact, structural/semantical, modular), and works at least one Example.

## Key Findings

- Polberg's normal-form translations all share a **fixed metric vocabulary**: full
  vs target-subclass, overlapping (non-injective), domain (argument/semantics)
  preserving vs altering, exact vs faithful vs strong vs weak, structural vs
  semi-structural vs semantical, and ⊕/⊗-modularity. Every Translation in this
  chunk is rated against exactly that vocabulary. *(passim, e.g., p.150, p.156,
  p.159, p.163, p.169, p.171, p.176, p.178, p.181)*
- All minimal/redundancy-free forms (EAFC, AFN, EAS, ADF) are non-modular. Most
  are generic, semantics-domain-preserving, exact, and structural (the ADF
  cleansed form is downgraded to **semi-structural** because constructing
  evaluations from in-mappings requires computation). *(pp.150, 152, 154, 158)*
- A strict hierarchy among ADF normal forms emerges: every **weakly valid** ADF
  is also **cleansed** but not vice versa (Theorem 4.2.23, p.169). Every **strongly
  valid** ADF is also weakly and relation valid (Theorem 4.2.41, p.184).
- The **strong-validity form for ADFs** sits adjacent to, but is strictly more
  restrictive than, the AADF⁺ subclass: every strongly valid D is an AADF⁺, and
  every redundancy-free cleansed AADF⁺ is strongly valid (Theorem 4.2.43, p.184).
  Strong validity additionally requires every argument to have an evaluation,
  which AADF⁺ does not. *(p.184)*
- Strong validity is structurally characterized: an AFN is strongly valid iff its
  induced support graph is **directed acyclic** (Theorem 4.2.34, p.181); an EAS is
  strongly valid iff its induced support graph is **rooted DAG with η as root**
  (Theorem 4.2.39, p.183); an ADF is strongly valid iff its **positive
  dependency graph** PDG^D is a DAG (Theorem 4.2.45, p.185).
- Polberg explicitly leaves the **ADF relation-valid translation** open: she
  shows two candidate constructions (reduct vs value-replacement) and gives
  examples where each fails, then declares this a future-work item (p.176, after
  Example 66). She likewise leaves **strong-validity translations** open for all
  three frameworks (AFN/EAS/ADF), proposing argument-duplication as the future
  approach (p.165, Example 69; p.176).
- Two distinct **consistency-form** translations are defined: *bypass* and
  *self-attacker*. Bypass is faithful at best (the bypass argument can appear in
  extensions). Self-attacker is exact at the cost of breaking stable semantics
  and being unusable for AFRA-style group-attack situations. *(pp.186, 191-192)*
- "Hidden" / "optional" support cycles motivate strong validity: weak + relation
  validity together do **not** eliminate them (Example 67-68, pp.177-178). The
  proposed strong-validity construction in EAS/ADF uses **argument duplication**
  rooted at η to flatten cycles (Example 69, p.179).
- Inconsistency origin O^a captures arguments that simultaneously support and
  attack a (Definition 4.2.48, p.187 in AFN; Definition 4.2.52, p.190 in EAS).
  Bypass arguments a^b are introduced for every a ∈ O^E, with a replacement
  function rep that re-routes supporting sets through bypasses.
- The **consistent EAF/EAFC subclass** is a subclass, not a normal form: the
  author has no translation into it (p.196). For EAFs strongly consistent and
  consistent coincide because EAFs allow only binary defense attacks (p.196).
- Subclass lemma 4.2.67 collects EAF(C) inclusions: NDef ⊊ BH ⊊ SCons, and
  NDef^EAFC ⊊ Bin^EAFC ⊊ Min^EAFC. *(p.199)*

## Definitions Introduced

### Definition 4.2.6 — EAFC Minimal Form *(p.150/137)*
Let `EFC = (A, R, D)` be an EAFC. The subframework `EFC^min = (A, R, D')` is
the **minimal form** of EFC, denoted `EFC^min ⊑^min EFC`, iff `D' ⊆ D` consists
of all and only elements `(T, a)` in D s.t. `∄T' ⊂ T, (T', a) ∈ D`.

### Definition 4.2.8 — AFN Minimal Form *(p.151/138)*
Let `FN = (A, R, N)` be an AFN. `FN^min = (A, R, N')` is the **minimal form**
of FN iff `N' ⊆ N` consists of all and only elements `(T, a)` in N s.t.
`∄T' ⊂ T, (T', a) ∈ N`.

### Definition 4.2.10 — EAS Minimal Form *(p.153/140)*
Let `ES = (A, R, E)` be an EAS. `ES^min = (A, R', E')` is the **minimal form**
iff R' ⊆ R (resp. E' ⊆ E) consists of all and only `(T, a)` in R (E) s.t.
`∄T' ⊂ T, (T', a) ∈ R(E)`.

### Definition 4.2.12 — ADF Redundancy-Free Form *(p.155/142)*
Let `D = (A, L, C)` be an ADF, `a ∈ A`, `C_a` its acceptance condition,
`E ⊆ par(a)` the set of all parents of a s.t. for every `e ∈ E`, `(e, a) ∈ L`
is **not redundant**. The **redundancy-free form** of `C_a`, denoted `C_a^E`,
is the reduct of `C_a` w.r.t. E. Then `D^r = (A, L^r, C^r)` is the
redundancy-free form of D, where `L^r ⊆ L` is the set of all non-redundant
links and `C^r = {C_a^E | C_a ∈ C, C_a^E is the reduct w.r.t. non-redundant
parents of a}`. Note: the reduct can be done with a "negative" reduct (removed
arguments assumed false; Defs 3.2.3, 4.2.3) or a "positive" one (assumed true,
[GRS15]); both produce the same result.

### Definition 4.2.14 — Cleansed Form *(p.157/144)*
Let `D = (A, L, C)` be an ADF. The **cleansed form** of D is the reduct of D
w.r.t. `A'`, where `A' ⊆ A` is the set of all and only arguments on A that
possess a standard evaluation on A.

### Definition 4.2.17 — AFN Weak Validity Form *(p.162/149)*
Let `FN = (A, R, N)` be an AFN and `A' = {a ∈ A | there exists a powerful
sequence for a on A}`. The trimmed full subframework `FN^A' = (A', R', N')`
of FN induced by A' is the **weak validity form** of FN, denoted
`FN^A' ⊑^wv FN`.

### Definition 4.2.19 — EAS Weak Validity Form *(p.164/151)*
Let `ES = (A, R, E)` be an EAS and `A' = {a ∈ A | there exists at least one
evidential sequence for a on A}`. The full subframework `ES^A' = (A', R', E')`
induced by A' is the **weak validity form** of ES.

### Definition 4.2.21 — ADF Weak Validity Form *(p.166/153)*
Let `D = (A, L, C)` be an ADF and `A' = {a ∈ A | a has an acyclic pd-evaluation
on A}`. The reduct `D^A' = (A', L^A', C^A')` of D w.r.t. A' is the **weak
validity form** of D, denoted `D^A' ⊑^wv D`.

### Definition 4.2.24 — AFN Relation Validity Form *(p.169/156)*
Let `FN = (A, R, N)` be an AFN and `FN^wv = (A', R', N')` its weak validity
form. Let `coh(X)` denote the set of all coherent subsets of `X ⊆ A'`. The
trimmed subframework `FN^rv = (A', R', N'')` of `FN^wv` is the **relation
valid form** of FN, denoted `FN^rv ⊑^rv FN`, iff
`N'' = {(X ∩ ⋃ coh(A' \ {a})) | (X, a) ∈ N'}`.

### Definition 4.2.27 — EAS Relation Validity Form *(p.173/160)*
Let `ES = (A, R, E)` be an EAS. The subframework `ES^rv = (A, R, E')` is the
**relation valid form** of ES, denoted `ES^rv ⊑^rv ES`, iff
`E' = {(X, a) ∈ E | there exists a self-supporting set S ⊆ A \ {a} s.t.
X ⊆ S}`.

### Definition 4.2.29 — ADF Relation Validity Form *(p.175/162)*
Let `D = (A, L, C)` be a redundancy-free ADF. D is in **relation valid form**
iff every minimal decisively-in interpretation of an argument `a ∈ A` can be
used in constructing an acyclic pd-evaluation for a. (No translation given.)

### Definition 4.2.30 — AFN Strong Validity *(p.179/166)*
Let `FN = (A, R, N)` be an AFN. With `sup(a) = ⋃_{C ⊆ A, CNa} C` (all
arguments supporting a) and `suf(a) = {S | S ⊆ sup(a) and ∀C ⊆ A s.t. CNa,
C ∩ S ≠ ∅}` (subsets of `sup(a)` that have an element in common with every
support set of a). FN is **strongly valid** iff for every function
`f : A → {S | a ∈ A, S ∈ suf(a)}` we can create a powerful sequence
`(a_0, ..., a_n)` consisting of all elements of A s.t. `f(a_0) = ∅` and
`f(a_i) ⊆ {a_0, ..., a_{i-1}}` for `i > 0`.

### Definition 4.2.35 — EAS Strong Validity *(p.181/168)*
Let `ES = (A, R, E)` be an EAS. ES is **strongly valid** iff for every
function `f : A → {S | a ∈ A, S ⊆ A, SEa}` we can create an evidential
sequence `(a_0, ..., a_n)` consisting of all elements of A s.t. `f(a_0) = ∅`
and for `i > 0` `f(a_i) ⊆ {a_0, ..., a_{i-1}}`.

### Definition 4.2.40 — ADF Strong Validity *(p.184/170)*
Let `D = (A, L, C)` be a redundancy-free ADF. D is **strongly valid** iff
every pd-function `pd_A^D` on A is sound and the set A can be ordered into
a pd-sequence of an acyclic pd-evaluation on A w.r.t. `pd_A^D`.

### Definition 4.2.46 — Consistent / Strongly Consistent AFN *(p.187/173)*
Let `FN = (A, R, N)` be a (set-form) AFN, `a ∈ A` an argument and `X ⊆ A`
the set of attackers of a. *a is **consistent*** iff `∄X' ⊆ X, X' N a`. *a
is **strongly consistent*** iff `∀B ⊆ A s.t. BNa, X ∩ B = ∅`. FN is
(strongly) consistent iff all of its arguments are.

### Definition 4.2.47 — Consistent / Strongly Consistent EAS *(p.187/173)*
Let `ES = (A, R, E)` be an EAS, `a ∈ A`, X the collection of arguments s.t.
`∃X' ⊆ X, X' R a` and B the collection of arguments s.t. `∃B' ⊆ B, B' E a`.
a is **strongly consistent** iff `X ∩ B = ∅`. ES is (strongly) consistent
iff all of its arguments are.

### Definition 4.2.48 — AFN Inconsistency Origin *(p.188/174)*
Let `FN = (A, R, N)` be an AFN and `a ∈ A`. The **inconsistency origin**
of a is `O^a = {b ∈ A | ∃B ⊆ A s.t. b ∈ B, BNa and bRa}`.

### Definition 4.2.49 — Replacement Function (AFN) *(p.188/174)*
Let `FN = (A, R, N)`, `a ∈ A`, `S ⊆ A`. The replacing arguments are
`P^b = {e^b | e ∈ S}`. The **replacement function**:
$$
rep(a, S, P^b, (B, a)) = \begin{cases}(B, a) & \text{if } B \cap S = \emptyset \\ (B', a) & \text{if } B \cap S \neq \emptyset, B' = (B \setminus S) \cup \{e^b | e \in B \cap S\}\end{cases}
$$

### Definition 4.2.52 — EAS Inconsistency Origin *(p.190/177)*
Let `ES = (A, R, E)` be an EAS, `a ∈ A`. The inconsistency origin of a:
`O^a = {b ∈ A | ∃B, B' ⊆ A s.t. b ∈ B ∩ B', BEa and B'Ra}`.

### Definition 4.2.53 — Replacement Function (EAS) *(p.190/177)*
Same shape as Definition 4.2.49 but for EAS support relation `(B, a) ∈ E`.

### Definition 4.2.60 — Consistent EAF *(p.197/184)*
Let `EF = (A, R, D)` be an EAF. EF is **(strongly) consistent** iff there is
no `x, y, z ∈ A` s.t. `(x, y) ∈ R` and `(x, (z, y)) ∈ D`.

### Definition 4.2.61 — Consistent / Strongly Consistent EAFC *(p.197/184)*
Let `EFC = (A, R, D)` be an EAFC. EFC is **consistent** iff there is no
`x, y, z ∈ A` s.t. `(x, y) ∈ R` and `({x}, (z, y)) ∈ D`. It is **strongly
consistent** iff there is no `x, y, z ∈ A` and `X ⊆ A` s.t. `(x, y) ∈ R`,
`x ∈ X` and `(X, (z, y)) ∈ D`.

### Definition 4.2.62 — Binary SETAF *(p.198/185)*
Let `SF = (A, R)` be a SETAF. SF is **binary** iff for every `E ⊆ A` s.t.
`∃a, (E, a) ∈ R` it holds that `|E| = 1`. `Bin^SETAF ⊆ Fr^SETAF`.

### Definition 4.2.64 — AFRA Recursion Depth *(p.198/185)*
Let `FR = (A, R)` be an AFRA. The **zero-depth conflicts** are
`R_0 = R ∩ (A × A)` (directed at arguments only). The **i-depth conflicts**
where `i > 0` are `R_i = R ∩ (A × R_{i-1})`. FR is of recursion depth i iff
`R = ⋃_{j=0}^i R_j`. `Rec_i^AFRA` denotes the subclass of AFRAs with recursion
depth i. If `i = 0`, FR is **non-recursive**.

### Definition 4.2.65 — EAF(C) without defense / symmetric attacks *(p.199/186)*
Let `EF = (A, R, D)` be an EAF(C). EF is **without defense attacks** iff
`D = ∅`. It is **without symmetric attacks** iff there are no `a, b ∈ A`
s.t. `(a, b), (b, a) ∈ R`.

### Definition 4.2.66 — Binary EAFC *(p.199/186)*
Let `EFC = (A, R, D)` be an EAFC. EFC is **binary** iff for every
`(a, (b, c)) ∈ D`, `|a| = 1`. `Bin^EAFC ⊊ Fr^EAFC`.

### Definition 4.2.68 — BAF Subclasses *(p.199/186)*
Let `BF = (A, R, S)` be a BAF. BF is **without support** iff `S = ∅`. It is
**support acyclic** iff the graph `(A, S)` is directed acyclic. BF is of
**support depth n** if all (simple) paths in `(A, S)` are of length at most n.

## Theorems / Propositions / Lemmas

### Theorem 4.2.7 — EAFC minimal form preserves all standard semantics *(p.150)*
For `σ ∈ {conflict-free, admissible, preferred, complete, grounded, stable}`,
`E ⊆ A` is a σ-extension of EFC iff it is a σ-extension of EFC^min.

### Theorem 4.2.9 — AFN minimal form preserves all (incl. coherent) semantics *(p.151)*
Same iff statement, σ ∈ {conflict-free, coherent, admissible, preferred,
complete, grounded, stable}. (Originally [PO14b, PO14a]; conflict-free and
coherent omitted from the formal statement of PO14b but are in its proofs.)

### Theorem 4.2.11 — EAS minimal form preserves all standard semantics *(p.153)*
σ ∈ {conflict-free, self-supporting, admissible, preferred, complete,
grounded, stable}.

### Theorem 4.2.13 — ADF redundancy-free form preserves both extension- and labeling-based semantics *(p.155)*
For σ ∈ {conflict-free, pd-acyclic conflict-free, model, stable, grounded,
acyclic grounded, xy-admissible, xy-complete, xy-preferred} with `x, y ∈
{a, c}`, E ⊆ A is a σ-extension of D iff it is a σ-extension of D^r. A
three-valued interpretation is a δ-labeling for δ ∈ {three-valued model,
admissible, preferred, complete, grounded} iff it is a δ-labeling of D^r.

### Theorem 4.2.15 — Cleansed form preserves extension-based semantics *(p.157)*
σ ∈ {conflict-free, pd-acyclic conflict-free, model, stable, grounded,
acyclic grounded, xy-admissible, xy-complete, xy-preferred}, x, y ∈ {a, c}:
σ-extensions of D and D^c coincide.

### Theorem 4.2.16 — Cleansed form labeling correspondence with f-completion *(p.158)*
If v is a σ-labeling of D for σ ∈ {three-valued model, admissible, preferred,
complete, grounded}, then `v|_{A'}` is a σ-labeling of D^c. If v is a
σ-labeling of D^c, then the **f-completion** of v to A is a σ-labeling of D.
Note the asymmetry: not every three-valued model or admissible labeling of
the original is recovered, because these semantics lack maximality and u-
completions would also be sufficient. The cleansed translation is therefore
*⊇-weak* under the three-valued model and admissible semantics.

### Theorem 4.2.18 — AFN weak validity preserves coherent and stronger semantics *(p.163)*
σ ∈ {coherent, strongly coherent, admissible, preferred, complete, grounded,
stable}: σ-extensions of FN and FN^wv coincide. Conflict-free is only weakly
preserved: if E is conflict-free in FN then `E ∩ A'` is conflict-free in
FN^wv; if E ⊆ A' is conflict-free in FN^wv then it is conflict-free in FN.

### Theorem 4.2.20 — EAS weak validity (analogous) *(p.164)*
σ ∈ {self-supporting, strongly self-supporting, admissible, preferred,
complete, grounded, stable}.

### Theorem 4.2.22 — ADF weak validity preserves aa-family semantics *(p.166)*
σ ∈ {pd-acyclic conflict-free, aa-admissible, aa-preferred, aa-complete,
acyclic grounded, stable}.

### Theorem 4.2.23 — Weakly valid ADFs are cleansed *(p.166)*
Every weakly valid ADF is also cleansed, but not vice versa.

### Theorem 4.2.25 — AFN relation validity preserves coherent and stronger *(p.169)*
For weakly valid `FN^wv`, σ ∈ {conflict-free, coherent, strongly coherent,
admissible, preferred, complete, grounded, stable}: σ-extensions of FN^wv
and FN^rv coincide.

### Theorem 4.2.26 — Composed AFN relation validity translation *(p.170)*
σ ∈ {coherent, strongly coherent, admissible, preferred, complete, grounded,
stable}: σ-extensions of FN and FN^rv coincide. Conflict-free is only weakly
preserved (same shape as Theorem 4.2.18).

### Theorem 4.2.28 — EAS relation validity preserves all standard semantics *(p.173)*

preferred, complete, grounded, stable} — note: even conflict-free coincides
exactly here, because EAS relation-validity translation is single-step (no
weakly-valid intermediate needed).

### Theorem 4.2.31 — AFN strong → weak ∧ relation validity *(p.180/167)*
If FN is strongly valid, then it is weakly and relation valid.

### Theorem 4.2.32 — Coherence in strongly valid AFNs *(p.180/167)*
Let FN be a strongly valid AFN. `E ⊆ A` is coherent iff for every `a ∈ E`
and set `C ⊆ A` s.t. `CNa, C ∩ E ≠ ∅`. (Validity of support drops out.)

### Theorem 4.2.33 — Universal-quantifier characterization of strong validity (AFN) *(p.180/167)*
FN is strongly valid iff there exists a sequence `(a_0,...,a_n)` of all
arguments in A s.t. given **any** function `f : A → {S | a ∈ A, S ∈ suf(a)}`,
`(a_0,...,a_n)` is a powerful sequence.

### Theorem 4.2.34 — Strong validity ⇔ DAG (AFN) *(p.181/167)*
Let `SG^FN = (A, N')` where `N' = {(a, b) | ∃E ⊆ A, a ∈ E s.t. ENb}` be
the support graph induced by FN. **FN is strongly valid iff `SG^FN` is a
directed acyclic graph.**

### Theorem 4.2.36 — EAS strong → weak ∧ relation validity *(p.182/168)*
Same shape as 4.2.31.

### Theorem 4.2.37 — Self-support in strongly valid EAS *(p.182/168)*
S ⊆ A is self-supporting iff for every `a ∈ S` there is a set `S' ⊆ S` s.t.
`S'Ea`.

### Theorem 4.2.38 — Universal-quantifier characterization (EAS) *(p.183/169)*
ES is strongly valid iff there exists `(a_0,...,a_n)` of all arguments in A
s.t. given any function `f : A → {S | a ∈ A, S ⊆ A, SEa}`, `(a_0,...,a_n)`
is an evidential sequence.

### Theorem 4.2.39 — Strong validity ⇔ rooted DAG with η root (EAS) *(p.183/169)*
Let `ES = (A, R, E)` with `A ≠ ∅` and `SG^ES = (A, E')` where
`E' = {(a, b) | ∃X ⊆ A, a ∈ X s.t. XEb}`. ES is strongly valid iff `SG^ES`
is a **rooted directed acyclic graph s.t. η is the root**. (The rootedness
contrast with AFNs is structural: EAS evidential sequences require a
supporting set for every non-η argument.)

### Theorem 4.2.41 — ADF strong → weak ∧ relation validity *(p.184/170)*
If D is strongly valid, then it is weakly and relation valid.

### Theorem 4.2.42 — pd-acyclicity in strongly valid ADFs *(p.184/170)*
`E ⊆ A` is pd-acyclic iff for every `a ∈ E` there exists a minimal decisively
-in interpretation `v_a` s.t. `v_a^t ⊆ E`.

### Theorem 4.2.43 — Strong validity vs AADF⁺ *(p.184/170)*
If D is strongly valid, then it is an AADF⁺. If D is a redundancy-free
cleansed AADF⁺, then it is strongly valid.

### Theorem 4.2.44 — Universal-quantifier characterization (ADF) *(p.185/171)*
For redundancy-free D: D is strongly valid iff there exists `(a_0,...,a_n)`
of all arguments in A s.t. given any pd-function pd on A,
`((a_0,...,a_n), ⋃_{i=0}^n pd(a_i))` is an acyclic pd-evaluation.

### Theorem 4.2.45 — Strong validity ⇔ PDG^D DAG (ADF) *(p.185/171)*
Let D be a redundancy-free, cleansed ADF and `PDG^D = (A, L')` where
`L' = {(a, b) | ∃v ∈ min_dec(in, b) s.t. a ∈ v^t}` its **positive dependency
graph**. D is strongly valid iff `PDG^D` is a directed acyclic graph.

### Theorem 4.2.56 — AFN self-attacker consistency form *(p.193/180)*
For FN^sc produced by Translation 15 and `E^b` the bypass set generated by
E ⊆ A in A': if E is coherent in FN, then `E ∪ E^b` is pd-acyclic in FN^sc;

## Algorithms / Procedures

### Algorithm — Translation 2: EAFC minimal form *(p.150)*
1. Input EAFC `(A, R, D)`.
2. Output `(A, R, D')` where `D' ⊆ D` consists of all and only `(T, a) ∈ D`
   s.t. `∄T' ⊂ T, (T', a) ∈ D`.

### Algorithm — Translation 3: AFN minimal form *(p.152)*
Same shape, applied to support relation N.

### Algorithm — Translation 4: EAS minimal form *(p.153)*
Apply minimality to both R and E.

### Algorithm — Translation 5: ADF redundancy-free form *(p.155)*
1. Input ADF `(A, L, C)`.
2. `L' ⊆ L` = links that are not redundant.
3. `C' = {C_a' | C_a ∈ C, C_a' = reduct of C_a w.r.t. non-redundant parents}`.

### Algorithm — Translation 6: ADF cleansed form *(p.158)*
1. `A' = {a ∈ A | a has standard evaluation on A}`.
2. Output reduct of `(A, L, C)` w.r.t. A'.

### Algorithm — Translation 7-9: weak validity (AFN/EAS/ADF) *(pp.162, 165, 169)*
For the respective framework: `A' = {a ∈ A | a has powerful sequence /
evidential sequence / acyclic pd-evaluation on A}`. Output trimmed/reduct.

### Algorithm — Translation 10: AFN relation valid (from weakly valid) *(p.169)*
1. Input weakly valid AFN `(A, R, N)`.
2. Compute `coh(X)` for `X ⊆ A`.
3. Output `(A, R, N')` where `N' = {(X ∩ ⋃ coh(A \ {a}), a) | (X,a) ∈ N}`.

### Algorithm — Translation 11: AFN relation valid (composed) *(p.171)*
`rv-Tr^AFN((A,R,N)) = rwv-Tr^AFN(wv-Tr^AFN((A,R,N)))`. Two-step.

### Algorithm — Translation 12: EAS relation valid *(p.173)*
Single-step: filter E to support sets reachable from a self-supporting set.

### Algorithm — Translation 13: AFN bypass consistency *(p.188-189)*
1. `A^b = {a^b | a ∈ O^E}` where `E ⊆ A` is the not-strongly-consistent set.
2. `A' = A ∪ A^b`.
3. For every `(B,a) ∈ N`, replace inconsistency-causing args via
   `rep(a, O^a, A^b, (B,a))` into `N'`.
4. For every `a ∈ O^E`, add support `({a}, a^b)` to N'.

### Algorithm — Translation 14: EAS bypass consistency *(p.190-191)*
Analogous; replaces support relation `(B, a) ∈ E`.

### Algorithm — Translation 15: AFN self-attacker consistency *(p.192-193)*
Same as Translation 13 but additionally adds `(a^b, a^b) ∈ R'` for every
bypass argument — bypasses self-attack.

### Algorithm — Translation 16: EAS self-attacker consistency *(p.194-195)*
Adds `({a^b}, a^b) ∈ R'` for every bypass argument.

## Semantics Defined / Compared

The chunk does **not** introduce new semantics; it carries the standard
extension- and labeling-based semantics defined earlier through every normal
form. Across the chapter the following distinct semantics names recur:

- **Conflict-free, admissible, complete, preferred, grounded, stable** — for
  every framework.
- **Coherent / strongly coherent** — AFN-specific (replaces "self-supporting"
  in EAS).
- **Self-supporting / strongly self-supporting / self-supporting conflict-free**
  — EAS-specific.
- **pd-acyclic conflict-free, model, acyclic grounded, xy-admissible,
  xy-complete, xy-preferred** with `x, y ∈ {a, c}`, **three-valued model
  (labeling), labeling admissible/preferred/complete/grounded** — ADF-specific.
- **aa-admissible, aa-preferred, aa-complete** — preserved by ADF weak
  validity (Theorem 4.2.22).

**Coincidences with AF-style semantics carried by translations:**
- Minimal forms (EAFC, AFN, EAS): preserve every named semantics in the
  family, σ-extensions of `F` and `F^min` are the *same set*.
- Cleansed ADF: preserves all extension-based semantics; for labeling-based
  three-valued / admissible semantics translation is **⊇-weak only** — some
  labelings are not retrieved by f-completion.
- Weak validity: preserves σ-extensions for σ ∈ {coherent, strongly coherent,
  admissible, preferred, complete, grounded, stable} (AFN); one-direction for
  conflict-free.
- Relation validity: same set as weak validity for AFNs after composition;
  EAS relation-valid is the only single-step single-iff translation that
  also preserves conflict-free.
- Strong validity: for AFNs/EAS this is structurally equivalent to a DAG
  (rooted DAG for EAS); for ADFs, strong validity ⊊ AADF⁺ ⊋ strong validity.
- Bypass consistency: all standard semantics preserved (AFN/EAS).
- Self-attacker consistency: all standard semantics preserved **except
  stable**, where one direction fails (`E^sc` stable → `E` stable but not
  vice versa) for AFN.

## Design Rationale

- **Why ADF redundancy-free form is generalized via reduct rather than
  filtering links:** The author explains that in SETAFs, removing an attack
  set does not eliminate the underlying parent-child relationship, but in
  ADFs the acceptance condition encodes it directly (p.154). Hence "redundancy
  -free" must operate on the condition itself, not just the link list.
- **Why cleansed form is semi-structural rather than structural:** Building
  the standard evaluation requires non-trivial computation (constructing
  decisive interpretations from in-mappings), so the cleansed translation is
  classified as semi-structural (p.158).
- **Why ADF cleansed labeling correspondence is asymmetric:** Three-valued
  model and admissible semantics lack maximality; u-completions would also
  be sufficient. The asymmetry is "caused by the used casting functions, not
  the fact that any labeling is really 'lost'" (p.158, Theorem 4.2.16).
  Alternative casting (u-completion) is signaled but not adopted.
- **Why use weakly valid form as a prerequisite for AFN relation validity:**
  Example 60 demonstrates that removing a self-supporting link in an AFN
  where `a` already lacks a powerful sequence makes `a` valid — undesirable.
  Hence weak validity must precede relation validity (p.170, after Example
  60).
- **Why no weakly-valid prerequisite for EAS:** "If an argument is not valid
  in the first place, removing a support relation is not going to help it
  anyway — only by adding a relation from a valid argument we can change
  the status of this argument" (p.171, Example 62).
- **Why ADF relation-valid translation is left open:** Two natural
  definitions both fail (Example 64-66, p.174-176). Reduct over decisively-
  in interpretations (AFN/EAS-style) cannot detect overpowering support
  redundancies. Value-replacement to *out* changes the condition's meaning
  (turning b from accepted into self-attacker). Decision: limit to
  redundancy-free ADFs via Definition 4.2.29 and leave the actual
  translation as future work.
- **Why argument duplication for strong validity:** Optional/hidden cycles
  (Examples 67-68, p.177-178) cannot be removed by weak/relation validity.
  Future plan: for each cycle-participating argument, introduce a duplicate
  copying all attacks/supports. The duplicate breaks the cycle while
  preserving extension behavior (Example 69, p.179, Figure 4.12).
- **Why strong validity = DAG (AFN) and rooted DAG with η root (EAS):**
  Theorems 4.2.34 and 4.2.39. This gives both *semantical* (universal
  quantifier characterizations 4.2.33/4.2.38) and *structural* readings of
  strong validity (p.180-183).
- **Why two consistency forms:**
  - **Bypass:** non-intrusive, applicable to non-standard semantics, only
    *faithful* (bypass appears in extensions).
  - **Self-attacker:** exact, but bypass cannot appear in extensions, so
    semantics that depend on universal attack (stable) break (p.192).
  The two are kept separate so the reader can pick based on which property
  they need.
- **Why no consistent EAF/EAFC translation:** EAFs allow only binary defense
  attacks; the AFN/EAS bypass machinery uses non-binary support. The author
  considers it future work and ships consistency only as a *subclass* (p.196).
- **Why non-modularity is acceptable:** All normal-form translations in this
  chunk are non-modular (`⊕`/`⊗`-modularity fails). The author argues this
  is a structural cost of guaranteeing semantic preservation: composing two
  redundancy-free / weakly valid / etc. frameworks can re-introduce the
  redundancy/invalidity that was removed locally (e.g., Translation 7 vs
  union of frameworks, p.150 explanation, p.154 explanation, p.169
  explanation). Modularity is not a goal here.

## Limitations Acknowledged

- **No ADF relation-valid translation** (only a definition + analysis of why
  two candidates fail). *(p.176)*
- **No strong-validity translations for any framework** (only definitions,
  semantic theorems, and the duplication sketch). *(pp.165, 176)*
- **Self-attacker consistency breaks stable semantics** in one direction
  (Theorem 4.2.56); not applicable to AFRA-style group-attack frameworks
  because attack propagation requires non-binary attack. *(p.192-193)*
- **Bypass consistency is only faithful, not exact** under standard
  semantics, because the bypass argument can appear in extensions. *(p.187)*
- **No EAF / EAFC consistency translation** exists yet — consistent EAF(C)
  is only a subclass. *(p.196)*
- **No BAF consistency normal form** in this chapter (deferred to Section
  4.7.4 where BAFs will not be translated into ADFs in the way other
  frameworks are). *(p.185)*
- **Weakly + relation validity does NOT imply no support cycles**;
  optional/hidden cycles remain (Example 67). Strong validity is needed.
- **EAS strong validity ≠ AFN strong validity structurally**: AFN ⇔ DAG;
  EAS ⇔ rooted DAG with η root. The asymmetry is intentional (every non-η
  EAS argument requires a supporting set) but distorts cross-framework
  intuition.

> extension–based semantics independent. It can be seen as verifying if an
> argument 'exists'." *(p.158, defending cleansed form's universality)*

> "We can observe that the concept of the strongly valid form in ADFs is
> actually quite close to the AADF⁺ subclass ... However, strong validity
> is in fact more restrictive, as it enforces the redundancy–freeness and
> the fact that every argument has to have an evaluation, which is not the
> case in AADF⁺." *(p.184)*

> "The bypass consistency form has the advantage of not affecting the attack
> relation and only changing the length of existing supports. Consequently,
> this normal form has also the chance of satisfying more demanding types
> of semantics, such as prudent or careful [CMDM05a, CMDM05b]." *(p.192)*

> "We thus leave obtaining a relation valid form translation and fine–tuning
> its definition as an open issue for the future work. However, please note
> that just like in EASs, we believe the translation will not have the
> weakly valid form prerequisite that is present in AFNs." *(p.176, ADF
> relation-valid is open)*

> "We are now ready to introduce the strongly valid normal forms for our
> frameworks and analyze their properties." *(p.179)*

## Implementation Notes

- **Normal-form pipeline order** (when implementing): minimal-form → redundancy-
  free / cleansed → weak validity → relation validity → strong validity →
  consistency form. Most translations are non-modular, so apply them
  monolithically per framework instance, never cumulatively to the union of
  two frameworks.
- **EAFC minimal form** is defined exclusively over defense-attack relation
  D minimality (subset-minimal targeting `(T, a)`). Implementation: enumerate
  `(T, a) ∈ D`; remove any `(T, a)` for which a proper subset `T' ⊂ T`
  satisfies `(T', a) ∈ D`. O(|D|² · |A|) naively.
- **ADF redundancy-free form** requires checking both attacker- and
  supporter-status of each link. The reduct construction over each
  acceptance condition is straightforward over a propositional / Z3 normal
  form: substitute `false` (negative reduct) or `true` (positive reduct) for
  removed parents and simplify.
- **Cleansed form** requires checking *standard evaluation existence* per
  argument; Polberg argues this is equivalent to checking for an `in`-
  mapping in the functional acceptance condition (Lemma 3.1.14 referenced).
  Implement as: for each argument a, search for any subset `S ⊆ par(a)` s.t.
  `C_a(S) = in`. If none, drop a and re-iterate.
- **Weak validity (ADF)** requires acyclic pd-evaluation existence per
  argument. This is reachable by topological sort over an induced positive-
  dependency graph; if no topological sort exists for the argument's
  dependents, remove it.
- **Relation validity (AFN)** is implemented in two stages (Translation 11):
  (1) compute weak validity, (2) for each support `(X, a)`, intersect X with
  the union of coherent subsets of `A' \ {a}`. Coherent-set enumeration is
  exponential — apply on small frameworks or use a reachability heuristic
  for the union.
- **Relation validity (EAS)** is single-step and cheaper. For each support
  `(X, a)`, check `∃ self-supporting S ⊆ A \ {a}` with `X ⊆ S`. This is
  an existential SAT-style check.
- **Relation validity (ADF)** is *not* implementable as given — Polberg
  leaves it open. Implementation should expose a flag and raise on
  unsupported.
- **Strong validity** can be detected structurally:
  - AFN: build `SG^FN`; check DAG (Tarjan's SCC).
  - EAS: build `SG^ES`; check rooted DAG with η root.
  - ADF: build positive dependency graph `PDG^D` (over redundancy-free,
    cleansed ADF); check DAG.
- **Strong validity construction** is left open for all three frameworks;
  the proposed approach is argument duplication. Implement as:
  1. Detect cycle-participating arguments via Tarjan SCC on the support
     graph.
  2. For each `a` in a non-trivial SCC, introduce a duplicate `a'` with
     copies of all attacks (in/out) and supports (in/out).
  3. Re-route one cycle-edge through the duplicate.
  4. Iterate until SCCs are trivial.
  5. Map extensions of the duplicated framework back by collapsing
     duplicates.
- **Bypass consistency form** (Translation 13):
  1. Compute inconsistency origins `O^a` for every argument.
  2. For `E ⊆ A` not strongly consistent, generate bypass set `A^b`.
  3. For every support `(B, a) ∈ N`, apply `rep(a, O^a, A^b, (B, a))`.
  4. For every `a ∈ O^E`, add `({a}, a^b)` to N.
- **Self-attacker consistency** is identical to bypass + adding self-attacks
  on bypasses. The R relation must be augmented with `(a^b, a^b)` for every
  bypass.
- **Subclass detection helpers** (Section 4.2.5):
  - SETAF binary: every attack set has cardinality 1.
  - AFRA depth: iterate `R_i = R ∩ (A × R_{i-1})` until fixed-point.
  - EAFC binary: every defense-attack first-component has cardinality 1.
  - BAF support depth: longest simple path in `(A, S)`.

For propstore, this argues a **layered presentation in the argumentation
layer**: every framework type carries a "normal-form pipeline" with each
stage as an opt-in transformer, plus a "subclass detector" returning a set
of subclass tags. Translations should be modeled as proposal artifacts (per
project doctrine — the source-of-truth must keep the original framework).
At render time, policy can elect which normal forms to apply.
