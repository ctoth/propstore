## Key Findings
- The chapter establishes a uniform classification scheme (Table 4.10, p.406) for translations between EAS / AF / SETAF / BAF / AFN / ADF along strength (cf, ssup, str ssup, adm, comp, pref, grd, stb), functional, syntactical, semantical, and computational axes. *(p.406)*
- Two modular EAS translations exist (to BAF Translation 77, to AFN Translation 78), both source-subclass; this gives a recurring meta-finding that modularity for support frameworks is harder than for attack-only structures. *(p.405)*
- ADF→AF for the ca₁/ca₂ families is genuinely problematic — three attempted approaches (relation removal, symmetric attack addition, breaker auxiliary arguments) are all shown to fail; only a partial ca₂ method (Translation 86) survives. *(p.422–p.425)*
- Strong impossibility result: no full or BADF/AADF⁺ source-subclass translation from ADF to AF can be exact under any analyzed semantics (cf, pd-acyclic cf, xy-admissible/complete/preferred, stable, model, three-valued model, labeling-{adm,comp,pref}). The reason is structural: ADFs handle SETAFs trivially, AFs do not. *(p.430, Theorem 4.10.6)*
- Labeling-based ADF→AF is preserved for complete/preferred/grounded but is only ⊆-weak for admissible because AF labelings can rate `out` more permissively than ADF. *(p.428–p.430)*

## Theorems / Propositions / Lemmas

### Theorem 4.9.8 *(p.401)*
For ES = (A, R, E) support-singular, attack-binary, strongly valid EAS, BF^ES from Translation 77 is support-acyclic and η ∈ A is the only argument such that no a satisfies aSη.

### Theorem 4.9.9 *(p.401)*
Tight semantic correspondence between EAS-side {cf, self-supporting, strongly self-supporting, admissible, preferred, complete, grounded, stable} and BAF-side {+cf, inverse-closed, +cf+inverse-closed, i-admissible, i-preferred, d-complete, d-grounded, stable} extensions, parametrized by R″ = {R^sec}.

### Theorem 4.9.10 *(p.403)*
For attack-binary EAS via Translation 78: strong-consistency only transfers AFN→ES one direction; if ES is all-supported and strongly consistent then FN^ES is strongly consistent. Minimality, weak/strong validity, relation validity propagate forward.

### Theorem 4.9.11 *(p.403)*
Evidential sequences in ES bijectively correspond to powerful sequences in FN^ES; self-supporting in ES ⇔ coherent in FN^ES.

### Theorem 4.9.12 *(p.404)*
For self-supporting (coherent) S, an argument a is acceptable w.r.t. S in ES iff defended by S in FN^ES.

### Theorem 4.9.13 *(p.404)*
Full extension correspondence for σ ∈ {cf, adm, pref, comp, grd, stb} between ES and FN^ES under Translation 78. (Strong / strongly self-supporting also correspond.)

### Theorem 4.9.14 *(p.408)*
For general EAS via Translation 79 (group attacks): strong-consistency is no longer two-way, but minimality / validity properties propagate forward like Translation 78.

### Theorem 4.9.15 *(p.408–p.409)*
Mapping of (strongly) self-supporting and σ ∈ {adm, pref, comp, grd, stb} extensions S of ES to S′ = S ∪ att(S) in FN^ES; reverse direction is via S = S′ ∩ A.

### Theorem 4.9.16 *(p.412)*
Translation 80 (strongly consistent EAS → ADF): the produced ADF D^ES is BADF; cleansed iff ES all-supported; redundancy-free iff ES all-supported and minimal; weak/relation/strong validity propagate; strongly valid + minimal ⇒ AADF⁺ strongly valid.

### Theorem 4.9.17 *(p.412)*
S is self-supporting in ES iff S is pd-acyclic in D^ES. Evidential sequences and acyclic pd-evaluations are in correspondence.

### Theorem 4.9.18 *(p.412)*
S is strongly self-supporting in ES iff pd-acyclic conflict-free in D^ES.

### Lemma 4.9.19 *(p.413)*
For self-supporting cf S: S attacks a or every set e-supporting a iff acyclic range v_S^a blocks every acyclic pd-evaluation of a in D^ES.

### Lemma 4.9.20 *(p.413)*
a acceptable w.r.t. S in ES iff a decisively in w.r.t. v_S^a in D^ES.

### Theorem 4.9.21 *(p.413)*
σ-extension correspondence between ES and D^ES for σ ∈ {adm, pref, comp} (mapping to aa-σ), stable to stable, grounded to acyclic-grounded.

### Theorem 4.9.22 *(p.417)*

### Theorem 4.9.23 *(p.417)*

### Theorem 4.10.1 *(p.420–p.421)*

### Theorem 4.10.2 *(p.426)*

### Theorem 4.10.3 *(p.431)*

### Theorem 4.10.9 *(p.448)*

## Algorithms / Procedures

### Translation 77 — EAS → BAF *(p.400)*
1. For each (a,b) ∈ R (singular attacks), put (a,b) in R'.
2. For each (X,b) ∈ E with b some argument and a ∈ X, put (a,b) in S.
3. Resulting BAF preserves arguments; no new arguments introduced.

### Translation 78 — Attack-binary EAS → AFN *(p.402)*
1. For each (a,b) with ({a},b) ∈ R, put (a,b) in R'.
2. For each a ≠ η with Z = {Z_i | Z_i E a}: if Z = ∅, add ({a}, a) to N (self-support); else add (Z', a) for every Z' ⊆ ⋃ Z_i with Z' ∩ Z_i ≠ ∅ for all i.

### Translation 79 — General EAS → AFN with group attacks *(p.408)*
1. att(A) := subsets of A of size > 1 attacking some y in R; A' := A ∪ att(A).
2. R' := {(X, y) | (X, y) ∈ R or ({X}, y) ∈ R} (lifted relation).
3. N := same as Translation 78 but using A'.

### Translation 80 — Strongly consistent EAS → ADF *(p.411)*
1. For arguments a, b: if some Z ⊆ A satisfies Z E b or Z R b, add (a, b) to L for every a ∈ Z; if no such Z exists and b ≠ η, add (b, b) to L.
2. Compute attack subformula att_a from sets B_i R a.
3. Compute support subformula sup_a from sets Z_i E a (DNF of conjunctions).
4. C_a := att_a ∧ sup_a, with C_η = ⊤ and sup_a = a (self-support) if Z = ∅, a ≠ η.

### Translation 81 — General EAS → ADF *(p.415)*
1. Apply Translation 14 or 16 to ES to obtain strongly consistent ES^{sc}.
2. Apply Translation 80 to ES^{sc}.

### Translation 82 — ADF → AF (aa-semantics) *(p.420)*
1. A' := {(F, B) | (F, B) minimal acyclic pd-evaluation for some a ∈ A on A}.
2. Attack: (F, B) → (F', B') iff B' ∩ F ≠ ∅.

### Translation 83 — ADF → AF (ac-semantics) *(p.424)*
1. A^a := minimal acyclic pd-evaluations.
2. A^c := minimal standard evaluations that cannot be made acyclic.
3. A' := A^a ∪ A^c; every element of A^c is a self-attacker; cross attacks via blocking.

### Translation 85 — ADF → AF (cc-semantics, breakers) *(p.433)*
1. A^{ev} := minimal partially acyclic pd-evaluations.
2. A^b := one breaker per argument appearing in some F.
3. Attack rules: ev→ev via blocking; breaker self-attack; bidirectional ev↔breaker links per F vs F∪G membership.

### Translation 86 — ADF → AF (ca₂-semantics) *(p.437)*
Same as Translation 85 but breakers are NOT self-attackers (independence required for labeling).

### Translation 87 — ADF → SETAF (cc-semantics) *(p.444)*
1. Same A^{ev} and A^b as Translation 85; breakers are sets F^b indexed by F.
2. Attacks include singleton {(F,G,B)} attacking another evaluation, {F^b} self-attack, evaluations ↔ breakers via F vs F∪G, plus group-attack edges from minimal sets of evaluations whose unions cover F to F^b.

### Translation 88 — ADF → SETAF (aa-semantics) *(p.448)*
1. Assume D is weakly valid.
2. R := {(E, a) | E ⊆ A minimal s.t. for every acyclic pd-evaluation (F, B) for a, E ∩ B ≠ ∅}.

## Semantics Defined / Compared
EAS-side: cf, self-supporting, strongly self-supporting, admissible, preferred, complete, grounded, stable.
BAF-side (paired in Theorem 4.9.9): +cf w.r.t. ∅, inverse-closed under S, +cf w.r.t. R″ ∧ inverse-closed under S, i-admissible, i-preferred, d-complete, d-grounded, stable.

ADF-side: pd-acyclic cf, aa-{adm, comp, pref}, acyclic-grounded, stable, ac-{adm, comp, pref}, grounded, cc-{adm, comp, pref}, model, ca₁/ca₂-{adm, comp, pref}, three-valued model, labeling-{adm, comp, pref, grd}.

## Comparisons With Prior Work
- Translation to BAFs avoids the CLS13 modification of BAFs because PO14b connects evidential and necessary support tightly. *(p.400)*
- AFN translations follow PO14b/PO14a for attack-binary EAS but generalize to group attacks via merging with Translation 29 from earlier in chapter 4. *(p.404, p.407)*
- ADF translation uses the "self-supporter" trick from AFN→ADF Translation 69; it is explicitly classified as "basic" not "modular" because of how acceptance conditions split between attack and support. *(p.413–p.414)*
- Coalition-based ADF→AF Translation 25 was weak under conflict-free; Translation 82 with evaluation arguments is strong because evaluations preserve information about pd-sets and blocking sets that pd-sets alone discard (Example 146). *(p.420–p.421)*
- The cc-family translation explicitly contrasts with the AFN/EAS defender translations (Translation 68, etc.) — breakers play a role analogous to "you are unsupported" but must self-attack. *(p.430–p.431)*
- The ca₁/ca₂ failure parallels the EAF section 4.6.2 difficulties (mentioned both in 4.10.1.4 setup and improvements). *(p.422)*

## Design Rationale
- **Why use evaluations as AF arguments instead of pd-sets:** "Two different evaluations that contribute the same arguments to a given extension can be attacked by different arguments and accidentally removing or merging them can affect both conflict-freeness and defense." Example 146 demonstrates the data loss. *(p.420)*
- **Why introduce a self-support link for unsupported arguments in EAS→ADF:** AFN treats unsupported arguments as valid; EAS treats only evidence-grounded arguments as valid. The self-link forces AF/ADF acyclic-range semantics to falsify them. The downside is that redundancy-free / minimal forms break, but acyclicity of semantics keeps the right behavior. *(p.412)*
- **Why use acyclic vs partially acyclic vs standard evaluations:** "homogeneous" aa-family allows the single acyclic kind on both inside and outside; ac splits with self-attacking standard evaluations because outside can attack but not be accepted; cc/ca need partially acyclic evaluations and breaker arguments because non-acyclic evaluations can still be accepted but must be defendable. *(p.422–p.430)*
- **Why breakers self-attack in cc but not in ca₂:** Self-attack ensures breakers cannot serve as defenders; in ca-semantics labeling-style independence between breakers is precisely the desired behavior. *(p.434, p.437)*
- **Why no exact ADF→AF translation possible (Theorem 4.10.6):** ADFs handle SETAFs trivially (Translation 31), AFs do not. Even restricted to BADFs or AADF⁺s, the SETAF embedding survives, so AF cannot capture all ADF semantics with identity casting. *(p.443)*
- **Why labeling-admissibility is only ⊆-weak (Example 153):** AF labeling can rate dependent arguments as `out` even when the ADF labeling cannot, because ADF admissibility checks Γ_D(v) compatibility while AF only checks defense by labeled set. *(p.442)*

## Limitations Acknowledged
- Translation 77 (EAS→BAF) restricted to support-singular, attack-binary, strongly valid EASs; Translation 78 to attack-binary; Translation 80 to strongly consistent. *(p.400, p.402, p.411)*
- Translation 78/79 break minimal form (additional self-supports for unsupported arguments). *(p.404, p.412)*
- Translation 79 weakens strong-consistency propagation (no longer two-way). *(p.408)*
- ADF→AF translations are at best faithful (not exact) outside grounded/acyclic-grounded; Theorem 4.10.6 shows this is unavoidable. *(p.443)*
- Translations 84 (faulty) and 86 (partial ca₂) demonstrate ca-family semantics resist straightforward AF reduction. *(p.428, p.437)*
- Labeling-admissibility translation is ⊆-weak only. *(p.442)*
- Footnote 19 (p.435) flags that minimal partially acyclic evaluations should ideally include both front and end pd-sequences; left for future work.
- AFRAs and EAF(C)s are excluded from the translation analysis. *(p.405)*
