# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

Replication is a central tenet of science. Recently, the replicability of scientific findings has been called into question, and it has been suggested that the rate of false findings is higher than previously assumed. The present study uses prediction markets to estimate the replicability of 44 studies published in prominent psychology journals and replicated in the Reproducibility Project: Psychology. The prediction markets predict the outcomes of the replications well and outperform a survey of the beliefs of individual researchers. Prediction market prices and surveys also provide valuable information regarding the expected relative effect sizes of the replications, where a positive replication is defined as having a p value of less than 0.05 in the same direction as the original study. This article argues that prediction markets could be used to obtain crowd-aggregated measures of the reproducibility of published research.

---

## Our Interpretation

The paper applies prediction markets to forecast which of 44 psychology studies will successfully replicate in the RPP, finding that market prices correctly predict 71% of outcomes and are well-calibrated as replication probabilities. This establishes prediction markets as a viable tool for assessing research credibility before costly direct replications. For propstore, the calibration result (market price as unbiased probability estimate) provides a model for how crowd-sourced beliefs can feed into the opinion algebra as properly calibrated evidence.
