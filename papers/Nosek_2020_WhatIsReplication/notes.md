# What is replication?

## One-Sentence Summary
This perspective paper redefines replication as any study for which any outcome would be considered diagnostic evidence about a prior claim, replacing the common but incorrect definition of "repeating a procedure to see if a finding recurs." *(p.1)*

## Problem Addressed
The common understanding of replication — repeating a study's procedure and observing whether the prior finding recurs — is intuitive, easy to apply, and incorrect. *(p.1)* This definition conflates procedural similarity with evidential diagnosticity, leading to confusion about what counts as a replication, what replication failures mean, and how replication relates to generalizability. *(p.2)*

## Key Contributions
- Proposes a new definition: replication is "a study for which any outcome would be considered diagnostic evidence about a claim from prior research" *(p.1)*
- Argues the common "repeat the procedure" definition is wrong because it emphasizes operational characteristics rather than interpretation of possible outcomes *(p.1)*
- Distinguishes replication from reproduction (re-analyzing original data) *(p.2)*
- Reframes the "direct" vs "conceptual" replication distinction as misleading — argues these are points on a continuum, not discrete categories *(p.2)*
- Introduces the generalizability space framework with four dimensions: Units, Treatments, Outcomes, Settings *(p.3)*
- Shows that replication and generalizability testing converge as theory matures *(p.5-6)*
- Argues replication value is strongest when existing understanding is weakest *(p.3)*

## Study Design
*Non-empirical paper — perspective/essay. No study design.*

## Methodology
Conceptual analysis and philosophical argument. The authors critique the standard definition of replication by showing it leads to paradoxes and ambiguities, then propose an alternative definition grounded in the diagnosticity of evidence for theoretical claims. They illustrate with examples from psychology and biology (Loewi's vagus nerve experiments). *(p.1-7)*

## Key Definitions

### Replication (proposed definition)
"A study for which any outcome would be considered diagnostic evidence about a claim from prior research." *(p.1)* This definition reduces emphasis on operational characteristics of the study and increases emphasis on the interpretation of possible outcomes. *(p.1)*

### Reproduction
"Reproduction is distinct from replication — it is re-analyzing the original data using the same analyses and confirming the same results." *(p.2)* Not a test of claims with new evidence.

### Replication reconsidered (critique of common definition)
The common definition — repeating a study's procedure and observing whether the prior finding recurs — fails because: *(p.2)*
1. It emphasizes the technical match between procedures rather than the evidential bearing of outcomes
2. It creates confusion between "direct" and "conceptual" replication as though these are meaningfully distinct categories
3. "Direct" replication and "conceptual" replication are not types of replication but points on a continuum of procedural similarity *(p.2)*

### Generalizability space
The set of all possible combinations of units, treatments, outcomes, and settings over which a claim might hold. *(p.3)* A single study examines one point in this space. Replication tests examine other points. *(p.3)*

### Four dimensions of generalizability *(p.3)*
- **U (Units):** The entities studied (people, cell lines, animal models, etc.)
- **T (Treatments):** What is administered or manipulated
- **O (Outcomes):** What is measured
- **S (Settings):** Context, time, location, conditions

## Methods & Implementation Details

### The "replication reduces" argument *(p.2-3)*
- In a replication, 2 things must be true: outcomes consistent with a prior claim would increase confidence in the claim, and outcomes inconsistent with a prior claim would decrease confidence in the claim *(p.2)*
- A study that can only confirm (not disconfirm) is not a replication — it fails the diagnosticity test *(p.2)*
- Prior claims with new evidence constitute replication; prior claims without new data (re-analysis) constitute reproduction *(p.2)*

### Why procedural similarity is insufficient *(p.3)*
- Individual studies do not provide comprehensive or definitive evidence about all conditions for observing evidence about claims *(p.3)*
- The generalizability space is large — variations in units, treatments, outcomes, and settings all matter *(p.3)*
- Claims are tied to contexts: a particular time of day, a particular point in history, a particular measurement method, a particular population *(p.3)*
- "Rarely do researchers limit their inferences to precisely those conditions" — they generalize beyond what the data strictly support *(p.3)*

### Replication as theory confrontation *(p.3-4)*
- Because there is no exact replication (every study has some difference), every replication test assesses generalizability to the new study's unique conditions *(p.3)*
- The generalizability space is large but not infinite because of theoretical constraints — some conditions should not affect the finding *(p.3)*
- Successful replication provides evidence of generalizability across conditions that inevitably differ from the original *(p.3)*
- Unsuccessful replication indicates reliability may be more constrained than recognized previously *(p.3)*

### Discovery vs. Mature Understanding spectrum *(p.4-5)*
- At **discovery** stage: weak theory, large potential generalizability space, replication has high value because theory doesn't specify boundary conditions *(p.5)*
- At **mature understanding** stage: strong theory, small remaining generalizability space, replication and generalizability testing converge *(p.5)*
- A complete theoretical account eliminates generalizability space because theoretical expectations are so clear that all tests are replication tests *(p.6)*
- With repeated failures, generalizability and replicability space both shrink until the theory makes no commitments to replicability *(p.6)*

### The Loewi example *(p.6)*
- Otto Loewi's vagus nerve experiment: original study used winter frogs *(p.6)*
- Replication attempts with summer frogs failed due to seasonal sensitivity of frog heart to acetylcholine *(p.6)*
- "Failures to replicate" were actually identifying a boundary condition (seasonality), not disproving the phenomenon *(p.6)*
- Demonstrates that replication failures advance theory by revealing previously unrecognized constraints *(p.6)*

### "Replication is rare" *(p.7)*
- The term "conceptual replication" has been applied to studies that use different methods to test the same question as a prior study *(p.7)*
- "Conceptual replications" are often generalizability tests — they are interpretive, at most, at identifying boundary conditions *(p.7)*
- A full assessment of whether one is testing replicability or generalizability is nuanced — would an outcome inconsistent with prior findings cause one to lose confidence in the theoretical claim? If so, it is a generalizability test *(p.7)*

### Ambiguity in "failure to replicate" *(p.7)*
- There is an irreducible ambiguity in failures to replicate: was the original evidence a false positive, or is the replication outcome a false negative, or have conditions changed? *(p.4)*
- "We can never know for certain that earlier evidence was a false positive... It is always possible to accumulate enough failed replications that it is not necessary to replicate externally. But that does not mean that all claims are true, and science cannot be self-correcting." *(p.4)*

## Results Summary
This is a perspective paper without empirical results. The key argumentative result is that the proposed definition (diagnosticity-based) resolves paradoxes created by the common definition (procedure-based), including: *(p.2-4)*
1. The false dichotomy between "direct" and "conceptual" replication
2. The confusion about what replication failures mean
3. The inability to decide whether something is a replication when procedures differ
4. The conflation of replication with reproduction

## Limitations
- The authors acknowledge that because replication is defined based on theoretical expectations, not everyone will agree that one study is a replication of another *(p.3)*
- "It is not always possible to make precise commitments to the diagnosticity of a study as a replication, for the simple reason that study outcomes are already known" *(p.3)*
- The definition requires judgment about what constitutes "diagnostic evidence," which may itself be contested *(p.7)*

## Arguments Against Prior Work
- Critiques the "common understanding" definition of replication as "repeating a study's procedure and observing whether the prior finding recurs" — calls it "intuitive, easy to apply, and incorrect" *(p.1)*
- Argues that the direct/conceptual replication distinction is misleading: "we argue that distinctions between 'direct' and 'conceptual' are of least relevance and possibly counterproductive for understanding replication" *(p.2)*
- Criticizes procedural definitions as failing to meet the criteria needed to qualify as a replication because they emphasize operational matching rather than evidential diagnosticity *(p.2)*
- Notes that purely procedural definitions lead to absurdities: "it is not possible to recreate an earthquake, a supernova, the Pleistocene, or an election" — yet we can still test claims about these phenomena *(p.2)*

## Design Rationale
- The diagnosticity-based definition was chosen because it shifts focus from "did you do the same thing?" to "would any outcome tell us something about the claim?" *(p.1)*
- This reframing resolves the problem that no replication is ever truly "exact" — all replications inevitably differ in some aspects *(p.3)*
- The framework deliberately avoids binary classification of studies as "replications" or "not replications" — instead placing them on a continuum of diagnosticity *(p.2-3)*

## Testable Properties
- Any study where outcomes are diagnostic evidence about a prior claim qualifies as a replication, regardless of procedural similarity *(p.1)*
- Successful replication must provide evidence of generalizability across conditions that differ from the original study *(p.3)*
- As theory matures, the distinction between replication and generalizability testing should converge (generalizability space shrinks) *(p.5-6)*
- Replication failures should more often reveal boundary conditions than disprove phenomena entirely (the Loewi pattern) *(p.6)*
- The value of replication should be inversely related to theoretical maturity — highest value at discovery stage *(p.3, p.5)*
