## Key Citations for Follow-up

- **Ioannidis (2005)** "Why most published research findings are false" — Theoretical framework predicting low replication rates based on study design, bias, and the ratio of true to false hypotheses. Essential complement to this empirical study.
- **Camerer et al. (2016)** "Evaluating replicability of laboratory experiments in economics" — Extends the replication approach to economics, providing cross-discipline comparison. Found 61% replication rate (vs 36% in psychology).
- **Klein et al. (2014)** "Many Labs" replication project — Large-scale multi-site replication of 13 classic effects, finding most replicated but with effect size heterogeneity.
- **Simmons, Nelson & Simonsohn (2011)** "False-positive psychology" — Demonstrates how researcher degrees of freedom inflate false positive rates, providing the mechanistic explanation for non-replication.
- **Button et al. (2013)** "Power failure" — Documents systematic underpowering in neuroscience, a key mechanism for non-replication that generalizes across disciplines.
