# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

Many landmark findings in preclinical oncology research are not reproducible, in part because of inadequate cancer cell lines and animal models. C. Glenn Begley and Lee M. Ellis propose how methods, publications and incentives must change if patients are to benefit.

---

## Our Interpretation

The authors report that Amgen scientists could reproduce only 6 of 53 (11%) landmark preclinical cancer studies over a decade of validation efforts, attributing failures to inadequate models, cherry-picked data, lack of blinding, and a publication culture that rewards novelty over robustness. They propose systemic reforms spanning methods (better cell lines and animal models), publications (mandatory negative data reporting), and incentives (credit for reproducibility over novelty). This is foundational evidence for calibrating uncertainty in claims derived from preclinical biomedical literature.
