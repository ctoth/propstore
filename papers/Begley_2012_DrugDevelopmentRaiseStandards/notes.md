# Drug development: Raise standards for preclinical cancer research

## One-Sentence Summary
A landmark commentary reporting that only 6 out of 53 (11%) landmark preclinical cancer studies could be reproduced by Amgen scientists, proposing specific methodological, publication, and incentive reforms to improve reproducibility.

## Problem Addressed
Many landmark findings in preclinical oncology research are not reproducible, in part because of inadequate cancer cell lines and animal models. Trials in oncology have the highest failure rate compared to other therapeutic areas. Given the high unmet need in oncology, it is understandable that barriers to clinical development may be lower than for other disease areas, and a large number of drugs enter oncology trials. However, the low success rate is not sustainable for establishing robust pipelines. *(p.0)*

## Key Contributions
- Reports the striking finding that scientists at Amgen could confirm the findings of only 6 of 53 (11%) landmark preclinical cancer studies over a decade of work *(p.0)*
- Identifies specific factors responsible for irreproducibility: inadequate cancer cell lines and animal models, cherry-picked data, lack of blinding, failure to repeat experiments, failure to show all data *(p.0-1)*
- Proposes concrete reforms: mandatory reporting of negative data, blinding, use of proper controls, better cell line characterization, more robust preclinical tumour models *(p.2)*
- Reports a secondary dataset: when examining reproducibility by citation count, non-reproduced articles had higher mean citation counts than reproduced ones *(p.1)*

## Study Design (empirical papers)
- **Type:** Retrospective validation study (attempted reproduction of published findings) *(p.0)*
- **Population:** 53 landmark preclinical cancer studies published over ~10 years, selected by Amgen haematology and oncology researchers as important for Amgen's drug development pipeline *(p.0-1)*
- **Intervention(s):** Attempted exact replication of published experiments by Amgen scientists, sometimes in collaboration with original investigators *(p.1)*
- **Comparator(s):** Original published results *(p.1)*
- **Primary endpoint(s):** Binary: could the key findings be confirmed or not *(p.1)*
- **Secondary endpoint(s):** Relationship between reproducibility and citation count *(p.1)*
- **Follow-up:** N/A — retrospective validation *(p.1)*

## Methodology
Over the past decade, Amgen scientists (in haematology and oncology) tried to reproduce published findings of 53 landmark preclinical cancer studies. These were papers selected because they reported a new drug target, biomarker, or combination technology with potential clinical applicability. In some cases, they collaborated directly with original authors. Where findings could not be reproduced, original authors had often been unable to reproduce their own findings. The results were binary: confirmed or not confirmed. *(p.0-1)*

## Methods & Implementation Details
- Selection criteria: "landmark" studies were those reporting a new drug target, biomarker, or technology that generated substantial follow-up and influenced the field *(p.0-1)*
- Validation approach: attempted exact replication, sometimes in collaboration with original authors *(p.1)*
- When results could not be reproduced, original authors often could not reproduce their own findings *(p.1)*
- Authors note that Amgen's findings are consistent with those of others in industry, citing Bayer HealthCare's report that only ~25% of published preclinical studies could be validated *(p.1)*
- Notably, published cancer research represented 70% of the studies analyzed in a report, some of which might overlap with the 53 papers examined at Amgen *(p.1)*
- The table on reproducibility vs. citations shows that non-reproduced articles from journals with impact factor >20 had mean citations of 248 (range 3-800) vs. 231 (range 82-519) for reproduced ones *(p.1)*

## Results Summary
Of 53 landmark preclinical cancer studies examined by Amgen scientists over approximately a decade, only 6 (11%) could be confirmed. This is consistent with a Bayer HealthCare study reporting ~25% reproducibility. Non-reproduced papers had comparable or higher citation counts than reproduced ones, indicating that citation count is not a proxy for reproducibility. The authors identify the "culture of oncology research" — emphasis on novelty over robustness — as a root cause. *(p.0-1)*

## Limitations
- The specific 53 papers are not identified, preventing independent verification of the claims *(p.1)*
- Binary outcome (confirmed/not confirmed) does not capture partial reproducibility *(p.1)*
- Selection was by Amgen scientists for papers relevant to their pipeline, introducing potential selection bias *(p.1)*
- No formal statistical analysis of the relationship between reproducibility and citation counts *(p.1)*
- The definition of "landmark" is subjective *(p.0)*

## Arguments Against Prior Work
- Current preclinical research culture prioritizes novelty and "stunning" results over reproducibility and robustness *(p.0)*
- Reviewers and editors do not demand sufficient standards of evidence: reagents, repeat experiments under the authors' direction, occasional replication in independent labs *(p.1)*
- Cell line characterization in published studies is often inadequate — many studies use cancer cell lines that are poorly characterized or have drifted from their original phenotype *(p.0)*
- Animal models used are frequently inadequate representations of human tumour environments *(p.2)*
- The incentive structure rewards publication of positive, novel findings and penalizes negative results, creating systematic bias *(p.2)*

## Design Rationale
- Authors argue for a multi-pronged reform approach because no single intervention addresses the systemic problem *(p.2)*
- Recommend mandatory negative data reporting because suppression of negative results distorts the evidence base *(p.2)*
- Advocate blinding and proper controls because their absence is a primary source of false positives *(p.1)*
- Propose better cell line characterization because poorly characterized cell lines propagate artifacts *(p.0)*
- Suggest more robust preclinical tumour models (e.g., genetically characterized, patient-derived xenografts) because standard models poorly predict clinical outcomes *(p.2)*

## Recommendations (from authors)
1. There must be more opportunities to present negative data; it should be the expectation that negative preclinical data will be presented at conferences and in publications *(p.2)*
2. Preclinical investigators should be required to report all findings, regardless of outcome *(p.2)*
3. Funding agencies, reviewers, and journal editors must agree that negative data can be just as informative as positive data *(p.2)*
4. Journal editors must play an active part in enforcing a cultural change — there must be mechanisms to report negative data accessible through PubMed or other search engines *(p.2)*
5. Funding organizations must recognize and embrace the need for new cancer research tools and assist in their development, and in providing greater community access to those tools *(p.2)*
6. Include support for establishing large cancer cell line collections with key investigator access (a simple, universal material transfer agreement), capabilities for genetic characterization of newly derived tumour cell lines and xenografts, identification of patient-specific genetic biomarkers, and generation of more robust, predictive preclinical tumour models *(p.2)*
7. Greater dialogue should be encouraged between physicians, scientists, patient advocates, and patients *(p.2)*
8. Physicians need better knowledge of the challenges and limitations of preclinical studies *(p.2)*
9. Institutions and committees should give more credit for teaching and mentoring, relying solely on publications or top-tier journals as the benchmark for promotion or grant funding can be misleading *(p.2)*

## Building a Stronger Standard (from authors)
- What reasons underlie the publication of erroneous, selective, or irreproducible data? The academic system and peer-review process tolerates and perhaps even inadvertently encourages such conduct *(p.2)*
- Studies should not be published using a single cell line or model, but should include a number of well-characterized cancer cell lines that are representative of the intended patient population *(p.2)*
- Cancer researchers must commit to making the difficult, time-consuming and costly transition towards new research tools, as well as adopting new robust, predictive tumour models and improved validation strategies *(p.2)*

## Testable Properties
- Only ~11% (6/53) of landmark preclinical cancer studies selected by Amgen were reproducible *(p.0)*
- Non-reproduced articles have comparable or higher citation counts than reproduced ones *(p.1)*
- Studies published in higher-impact-factor journals (>20) were not more reproducible than those in lower-impact-factor journals (5-19) *(p.1)*
- Bayer HealthCare independently found ~25% reproducibility rate for published preclinical studies *(p.1)*
- Oncology clinical trials have the highest failure rate among therapeutic areas *(p.0)*
