# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

Any experiment brings about results and conclusions that seemingly have a component of uncertainty. Many factors influence that degree of that uncertainty, yet they can be overlooked when drawing conclusions from a body of evidence. Here, we discuss how subjective logic and the concept of a subjective opinion can provide empirical scientists with the chance to reason about this uncertainty into the answer that researchers seek to provide to their research questions. We illustrate this approach by focusing on a body of evidence already meta-analysed, as from this starting point we can show how each finding can be encoded as a subjective opinion, and how findings can be aggregated to produce an answer that explicitly accounts for the chosen sources of uncertainty. We show that the encoding of empirical findings can be done explicitly and by modifying findings as subjective opinions, providing researchers with the chance to reason explicitly about first-order (relative to the distribution of probability mass) and second-order (relative to the incertitude of distributing the probability mass) uncertainty. Subjective logic promises to be a powerful complementary tool to incorporate uncertainty explicitly and transparently in the evaluation of research.

## Our Interpretation

The paper addresses the problem that traditional meta-analysis collapses multiple sources of uncertainty into a single pooled estimate, losing information about *why* findings are uncertain. By encoding each finding as a Josang subjective opinion (belief, disbelief, uncertainty, base rate) and fusing via Weighted Belief Fusion, the method transparently decomposes uncertainty into factors (sample size, CI width, replication status, peer review, same-source bias). This is directly relevant to propstore because it validates the opinion algebra approach already implemented and demonstrates how per-factor uncertainty decomposition can be applied to real empirical data aggregation.
