# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

John Pollock (1940-2009) was an influential American philosopher who made important contributions to various fields, including epistemology and cognitive science. In the last 25 years of his life, he also contributed to the computational study of defeasible reasoning and practical cognition in artificial intelligence. He developed one of the first formal systems for argumentation-based inference. This paper presents an appreciation of Pollock's work on defeasible reasoning and argumentation, discussing both its merits and some problematic aspects. It also discusses the place of his work in the broader context of computational argumentation; while innovative, his work contains important lessons for current research on formal models of argumentation.

---

## Our Interpretation

This paper reviews the work of philosopher John Pollock on computational argumentation following his death in 2009, examining how his formalisms for defeasible reasoning (inference graphs, defeat types, argument strength) anticipated and influenced the field. The key finding is that while Pollock worked in relative isolation from the AI argumentation community, many of his core ideas (undercutting defeat, inference graphs over trees, partial defeat status) became foundational to modern frameworks like ASPIC+. Relevant as essential background for understanding the theoretical foundations and design decisions behind any computational argumentation system.
