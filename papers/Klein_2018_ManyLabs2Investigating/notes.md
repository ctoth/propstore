# Many Labs 2: Investigating Variation in Replicability Across Samples and Settings

## One-Sentence Summary
A massive preregistered replication project that tested 28 classic and contemporary psychological findings across 125 samples in 36 countries (N=15,305), finding that 54% replicated at p<.05 with median effect sizes roughly one-quarter of originals, and that variation in replicability was driven primarily by which effect was studied rather than by sample or setting characteristics.

## Problem Addressed
Prior to Many Labs 2, it was unclear whether failures to replicate psychological findings were due to (a) the original effects being false positives or inflated by analytic flexibility, (b) hidden moderators tied to specific samples or settings, or (c) methodological differences between original and replication studies. Many Labs 1 (Klein et al., 2014) had examined 13 effects but used a convenience sample of mostly WEIRD populations. ML2 expanded this by using a formal adversarial collaboration design with preregistered protocols, peer-reviewed in advance, to disentangle effect-level replicability from sample/setting-level variation. *(p.3-4)*

## Key Contributions
- Largest coordinated replication effort at time of publication: 28 effects, 125 samples, 36 countries, 15,305 participants *(p.0)*
- Demonstrated that 54% of effects replicated at p<.05, 50% at p<.0001 *(p.3)*
- Median replication Cohen's d = 0.15 vs. median original d = 0.60; 75% of replications yielded smaller effects, 32% were in the opposite direction *(p.3)*
- Heterogeneity was primarily attributable to the effect being studied, not to sample or setting characteristics *(p.28)*
- Little evidence for WEIRD vs. non-WEIRD moderation of effect sizes *(p.28-29)*
- No significant moderation by lab vs. online administration or task order *(p.28)*
- Only 1 of 28 effects showed tau > .20 (moderate heterogeneity); 8 had tau near .10 (slight) *(p.28)*

## Study Design
- **Type:** Preregistered multi-site replication (crowdsourced collaboration) *(p.4-5)*
- **Population:** N = 15,305 participants from 125 samples across 36 countries and territories; approximately half of samples assigned to each protocol (Slate 1 vs Slate 2) *(p.5, p.8)*
- **Intervention(s):** Each participant completed approximately half of the 28 effects (14 per slate), administered online via Qualtrics *(p.5)*
- **Comparator(s):** Within-effect comparison of replication effect size to original effect size; between-sample comparison of effect heterogeneity *(p.5)*
- **Primary endpoint(s):** Cohen's d (or equivalent standardized effect size) for each of 28 effects, compared to original effect size *(p.5)*
- **Secondary endpoint(s):** Heterogeneity statistics (Q, tau, I²), moderator analyses (WEIRD vs. non-WEIRD, lab vs. online, task order), replication success rates under multiple criteria *(p.25-37)*
- **Follow-up:** No longitudinal follow-up; cross-sectional data collection over ~6 months in 2014-2015 *(p.5-8)*

## Methodology

### Effect Selection
Effects were selected via a formal process: candidates nominated on an open call, then evaluated by the project team and advisors on: (a) feasibility of implementation online, (b) importance/breadth of study, (c) variety of methodology, (d) not included in Many Labs 1 or other large-scale replications. Effects that had been repeatedly replicated were preferred. The final set included 13 classic (pre-2002) and 15 contemporary (post-2002) effects spanning social, cognitive, developmental, and judgment/decision-making psychology. *(p.4-5)*

### Protocol Design
Each replication protocol was designed in consultation with the original authors when possible. Changes from original protocols were documented in Table 1. All studies were administered online via Qualtrics rather than paper-and-pencil. Protocols were peer-reviewed before data collection. Key adaptations included: translating materials into local languages, converting currency/units, and adapting scenarios to local contexts. *(p.5-7)*

### Slate Assignment
The 28 effects were divided into two slates of 14 effects each. Each sample was randomly assigned to complete one slate. Effects within slates were presented in randomized order with demographics interspersed. The study took approximately 30 minutes. *(p.5)*

### Data Analysis
- Confirmatory analyses followed the Registered Report model (Chambers, 2013) *(p.5)*
- Effect sizes computed as Cohen's d with 95% CIs *(p.10)*
- Random-effects meta-analysis across sites for each effect *(p.25)*
- Cochran's Q test for heterogeneity, with tau and I² statistics *(p.28-29)*
- Moderator analyses: WEIRD score (continuous composite), lab vs. online, task order position *(p.28-33)*
- Replication success evaluated under multiple criteria: p<.05, p<.0001, original study's sample size power, 2.5x original sample, 50 per group, and minimum sample to detect effect at alpha=.05 *(p.34-37)*

### WEIRD Classification
Each sample was classified on five dimensions: Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic. Scores ranged from 0 to 5 based on country-level indicators. Median split used to create high-WEIRD and low-WEIRD categories. Data sources: UN Human Development Index, World Bank, Economist Intelligence Unit Democracy Index, UNESCO education data. *(p.28-29, p.31)*

## Key Equations / Statistical Models

$$
d = \frac{M_1 - M_2}{SD_{pooled}}
$$
Where: $M_1, M_2$ = group means, $SD_{pooled}$ = pooled standard deviation. Standard Cohen's d used throughout for between-group comparisons. *(p.10)*

$$
Q = \sum_{i=1}^{k} w_i (d_i - \bar{d})^2
$$
Where: $w_i$ = inverse-variance weight for study $i$, $d_i$ = effect size for study $i$, $\bar{d}$ = weighted mean effect size. Cochran's Q test for heterogeneity. *(p.28)*

$$
I^2 = \frac{Q - (k-1)}{Q} \times 100\%
$$
Where: $Q$ = Cochran's Q statistic, $k$ = number of studies. Percentage of total variability due to between-study heterogeneity. *(p.28)*

$$
\tau = \sqrt{\frac{Q - (k-1)}{C}}
$$
Where: $C$ = scaling constant derived from weights. Estimate of between-study standard deviation in true effect sizes. *(p.28)*

## Methods & Implementation Details
- All studies administered online via Qualtrics surveys *(p.5)*
- Materials translated to local languages; back-translated and reviewed *(p.8)*
- Currency and measurement units converted to local equivalents *(p.6-7)*
- Data quality checks: attention checks, speeding detection, completion criteria *(p.9)*
- Exclusion criteria: participants who did not complete the study, failed attention checks, or completed the study in under a threshold time *(p.9)*
- Random-effects meta-analysis using inverse-variance weighting *(p.25)*
- Cochran's Q, tau, I² computed for each effect across all samples *(p.28-29)*
- WEIRD score computed as sum of 5 binary indicators per country *(p.28)*
- Moderator analyses: meta-regression with WEIRD score, lab/online indicator, task order position as predictors *(p.28-33)*
- Confirmatory analyses pre-registered; exploratory analyses clearly labeled *(p.5)*
- Software: R, with metafor package for meta-analysis *(p.5)*
- Data, materials, and analysis scripts publicly available at https://osf.io/8cd4r/ *(p.5)*

## Results Summary

### Overall Replication Rates
15 of 28 (54%) effects replicated at p<.05; 14 of 28 (50%) at p<.0001. The high-powered design means the p<.05 and p<.0001 rates are nearly identical, reflecting genuine signal detection rather than marginal significance. *(p.3, p.34)*

### Effect Size Shrinkage
Median original d = 0.60; median replication d = 0.15. 21 of 28 (75%) replications yielded smaller effects than originals. 9 of 28 (32%) were in the opposite direction of the original. Only 7 (25%) yielded effects equal to or larger than the original. *(p.3)*

### Strongest Replications
The trolley dilemma (Effect 5: d = 2.50, replication d = 2.22) and moral foundations (Effect 12: d = 0.90, replication d = 0.45) showed the strongest replications. Effects with the largest original effect sizes tended to replicate more successfully. *(p.25-26)*

### Heterogeneity Analysis
11 of 28 (39%) effects showed significant heterogeneity (Q test). Most heterogeneity occurred among the effects with the largest overall effect sizes. Only 1 effect had tau > .20 (moderate heterogeneity). 8 effects had tau near .10 (slight heterogeneity). The remaining 19 effects showed minimal heterogeneity (tau near zero). *(p.28-29)*

### WEIRD vs. Non-WEIRD
A 2x2 ANOVA (high vs. low WEIRD x effect) showed minimal effect of WEIRDness. Exploratory comparisons revealed little heterogeneity between WEIRD and less-WEIRD cultures. Western countries were more likely to classify on the basis of family resemblance; East Asians were more likely to give rule-based responses (Effect 19, the only effect showing cultural moderation). *(p.28-29, p.19)*

### Lab vs. Online
No significant moderation by administration setting (lab vs. online). *(p.28)*

### Task Order
No significant moderation by position in the study (first vs. last). Table 4 shows minimal differences. *(p.33)*

### Variability Attribution
The primary finding is that variability in effect sizes was driven by which effect was studied, not by the sample or setting. This was tested via a 2x2 ANOVA structure and confirmed by the heterogeneity analyses. *(p.28, p.38)*

## Limitations
- All replications were online, which may not capture effects that require in-person interaction or specific lab equipment *(p.38)*
- Some effects required adaptations from original protocols (e.g., paper-and-pencil to online, language translation) that may have altered the manipulation *(p.6-7)*
- The 28 effects were not a random sample of psychology; they were selected for feasibility and importance, which may bias the replication rate upward or downward *(p.4-5)*
- WEIRD classification used a coarse 5-point composite; finer-grained cultural dimensions were not examined *(p.28-29)*
- Some effects had very small original sample sizes (e.g., N=27-77), making the original effect size estimates noisy *(p.35-37)*
- The study cannot distinguish between true null effects and effects too small to detect, even with the large samples *(p.38)*
- Multiple comparisons in the 28 moderator analyses were not corrected for; some significant results may be false positives *(p.38)*
- Task order was randomized within slates, but the number of preceding tasks was confounded with fatigue *(p.33)*

## Arguments Against Prior Work
- Challenges the "hidden moderator" explanation for replication failures: if effects depend on specific samples or settings, heterogeneity should be high, but it was generally low *(p.3, p.38)*
- Challenges the assumption that WEIRD samples produce fundamentally different results: little evidence for WEIRD vs. non-WEIRD differences *(p.28-29)*
- Argues against the interpretation that replication failures are due to methodological differences between original and replication studies, since protocols were peer-reviewed and approved by original authors *(p.5)*
- Critiques the practice of relying on small, underpowered original studies: the median original N was small relative to the effect sizes found *(p.35-37)*
- Challenges the view that lab vs. online administration is a meaningful moderator of psychological effects *(p.28)*

## Design Rationale
- Preregistration with peer review of protocols chosen to eliminate researcher degrees of freedom and ensure fidelity to original methods *(p.5)*
- Two-slate design chosen to balance participant burden (~30 min) against the number of effects tested *(p.5)*
- WEIRD composite score chosen as a simple, interpretable measure of cultural distance; acknowledged as coarse but sufficient for the primary question *(p.28-29)*
- Random-effects meta-analysis chosen over fixed-effects because the goal was to estimate variability across sites, not just average effect *(p.25)*
- Multiple replication success criteria used because there is no consensus on what constitutes a "successful" replication *(p.34)*
- Original authors consulted on protocol design to minimize the "straw man" critique *(p.5)*

## Testable Properties
- Replication effect sizes should be smaller than original effect sizes for the majority (>50%) of effects studied *(p.3)*
- Heterogeneity (Q statistic) should be concentrated among the largest effects, not among near-zero effects *(p.28)*
- WEIRD score should not significantly moderate effect sizes for the majority of effects *(p.28-29)*
- Lab vs. online administration should not significantly moderate effect sizes *(p.28)*
- Task order position should not significantly moderate effect sizes *(p.33)*
- The trolley dilemma (principle of double effect) should show the largest and most robust replication effect *(p.25)*
- Tau values should be < .20 for the vast majority of effects *(p.28)*
- Effects with original d > 0.50 should replicate at higher rates than effects with original d < 0.50 *(p.34)*
- The correlation between original effect size and replication effect size should be positive *(p.25)*
- I² values should indicate that most variability is within-study, not between-study *(p.28-29)*

## Relevance to Project
This paper is directly relevant to propstore's uncertainty and argumentation layers. It provides empirical base rates for calibrating belief in psychological claims: (1) roughly half of published effects may not replicate, (2) effect sizes are typically inflated by ~75% in original publications, (3) cross-cultural variation is generally small for most effects. These findings inform how much uncertainty to assign to published empirical claims in the argumentation framework, and support the design principle of holding multiple competing stances without premature commitment. The heterogeneity analysis methodology (Q, tau, I²) could inform how propstore assesses agreement vs. disagreement across sources.
