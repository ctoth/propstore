## Definitions and Notation

- **Inversion arguments** *(p.188, Eq. 10.35)*: input to multinomial conditional inversion is a set of conditionals `ω_{Y|X} = {ω_{Y|x_1}, ..., ω_{Y|x_k}}` together with a base-rate distribution `a_X` on X.
- **Inverted projected probability** *(p.189, Eq. 10.36)*: `P_{X|y_j}(x) = a_X(x) * P_{Y|x}(y_j) / Σ_{i=1..k} a_X(x_i) * P_{Y|x_i}(y_j)`. This is the multinomial generalisation of Bayes' theorem at the projected-probability level.
- **Dogmatic inverted opinion** `ω_{X|y_j}` *(p.189, Eq. 10.37)*: the simplest opinions satisfying Eq. (10.36). Defined by `b_{X|y_j}(x_i) = P_{X|y_j}(x_i)`, `u_{X|y_j} = 0`, `a_{X|y_j} = a_X`. Dogmatic = no uncertainty.
- **Proper inverted conditional** `ω_{X|˜Y}` (with tilde over the bar) — generally contains uncertainty, in contrast to the dogmatic versions.
- **Theoretical maximum uncertainty** `ü_{X|y_j}` *(p.189-190)*: the largest u that is consistent with both Eq.(10.36) (projection equality) and Eq.(3.12) (non-negativity of belief masses). Any uncertainty-maximised inverted opinion has at least one belief mass equal to zero.
- **Sum of conditional uncertainty** `u^S_{Y|X} = Σ_x u_{Y|x}` *(p.190, Eq. 10.42)*.
- **Proportional uncertainty weights** `w^u_{Y|x}` *(p.190, Eq. 10.43)*: `u_{Y|x}/u^S_{Y|X}` when `u^S_{Y|X} > 0`, else `0`.

- **Uncertainty of inverted multinomial conditional** `u_{X|˜y_j}` *(p.192, Eq. 10.50)*: `ü_{X|y_j} · ũ_{X|y_j}`. Range: `[0, ü_{X|y_j}]`.
- **Inverted conditional opinion** `ω_{X|˜y_j}` *(p.192, Eq. 10.51)*: `(b_{X|˜y_j}, u_{X|˜y_j}, a_X)` with `b_{X|˜y_j}(x) = P_{X|y_j}(x) − u_{X|˜y_j}·a_X(x)`.
- **Definition 10.6 (Multinomial Subjective Bayes' Theorem)** *(p.193)*: Operator symbol `~φ` (tilde-circled phi) for conditional inversion. `ω_{X|˜Y} = ~φ(ω_{Y|X}, a_X)` (Eq. 10.52).
- **Definition 10.7 (Multinomial Abduction)** *(p.193)*: Operator symbol `~⊚` (tilde-circled `⊚`). Two-step process: (1) invert with `~φ`, (2) deduce. `ω_{X||˜Y} = ω_Y ~⊚ (ω_{Y|X}, a_X) = ω_Y ⊚ ~φ(ω_{Y|X}, a_X) = ω_Y ⊚ ω_{X|˜Y}`. (Eq. 10.53-10.56)
- **Joint opinion** `ω_{YX}` *(p.199)*: opinion over the product variable `YX`. `Y` is child, `X` is parent.
- **Marginal opinion on Y** `ω_{[Y]}` (double-bracket Y), and on X `ω_{[X]}`. Marginal projected probabilities equal deduced projected probabilities `P_{[Y]} = P_{Y||X}`, but uncertainty masses generally differ due to approximate computation.
- **Joint base rate distribution** `a_{YX} = P_{Y|X} · a_X` *(p.201, Eq. 11.7)*: vector multiplication of conditionals and base rate of X.

## Operators Defined / Used in This Range (continued)

- **Multinomial Subjective Bayes' theorem** `~φ` *(Def 10.6, p.193)*: signature `(set of conditionals ω_{Y|X}, base rate a_X) → set of inverted conditionals ω_{X|˜Y}`. Generalises Bayes to opinions; preserves projected probabilities; increases uncertainty under MBR.
- **Multinomial Abduction** `~⊚` *(Def 10.7, p.193)*: signature `(evidence ω_Y, conditionals ω_{Y|X}, base rate a_X) → opinion ω_{X||˜Y}`. Composition: invert, then deduce.

## Quotes Worth Preserving

- *(p.193)*: "repeated inversion of conditionals with MBRs preserves the projected probabilities, and produces increased uncertainty masses in general. The increased uncertainty mass, and thereby the reduced confidence, is a natural consequence of conditional inversion. This effect is not visible for probabilistic conditional inversion, and demonstrates the advantage of the subjective Bayes' theorem over the probabilistic Bayes' theorem."
- *(p.194)*: "in the case of causal situations, it is normally easier to estimate causal conditionals than the opposite derivative conditionals" — motivates abduction over direct conditional reasoning.
- *(p.198)*: "the probability `P_X(x_1)=0.65` of the most likely plan x_1 has no belief support at all, and is only based on uncertainty, which would be worrisome in a real situation. A likelihood expressed as a scalar probability can thus hide important aspects of the situation."
- *(p.198)*: "ability of subjective logic to generate conclusions that reflect levels of confidence can be combined with the relatively qualitative process of ACH (analysis of competing hypotheses) ... to produce a formal and more quantitative framework for intelligence analysis."

## Definitions and Notation (continued)

- **Joint uncertainty mass** `u_{YX}` *(p.203, Eq. 11.13-11.15)*: proportional average. `u_{YX}/ü_{YX} = (u_{[X]}+u_{[Y]})/(ü_{[X]}+ü_{[Y]})`. So `u_{YX} = ü_{YX}(u_{[X]}+u_{[Y]})/(ü_{[X]}+ü_{[Y]})`. If `ü_{[X]}+ü_{[Y]}=0`, then `u_{YX}=0`. Soundness: `u_{[X]}+u_{[Y]} ≤ ü_{[X]}+ü_{[Y]}` ensures `u_{YX} ∈ [0,1]`.
- **Joint opinion** `ω_{YX} = (b_{YX}, u_{YX}, a_{YX})` *(Eq. 11.16)* with `b_{YX}((yx)_i) = P_{YX}((yx)_i) − u_{YX}·a_{YX}((yx)_i)`.
- **Joint product notation** `ω_{YX} = ω_{Y|X} · ω_X` *(Eq. 11.17)* — joining operation. Converges to proportional multiplication as dependence → independence.
- **Marginalisation onto X** *(Eq. 11.18-11.20)*: `P_{[X]}(x_i) = Σ_j P_{XY}(x_iy_j)`, similarly for `a` and `b`. `u_{[X]} = u_{[Y]} = u_{YX}` *(Eq. 11.21)* — marginal uncertainties equal joint uncertainty.
- **Marginal conditional opinion** `ω_{[Y|x]}` *(Eq. 11.22-11.23)*: `P_{[Y|x]}(y) = P_{YX}(yx)/Σ_{y_j} P_{YX}(y_jx)`; `u_{[Y|x]} = u_{YX}·ü_{Y|x}/ü_{YX}`.

- **Definition 12.1 (Correctness of Results)** *(p.209)*: degree to which results represent the true state of the situation modelled.
- **Definition 12.2 (Model Correctness)** *(p.211)*: model is correct for a class of situations when it consistently produces correct results in all instances of that class.
- **Three types of truth** *(p.209)*: ground truth (objectively observable), consensus truth (commonly shared opinion / norm), subjective truth (analyst's own).
- **Three types of result correctness** *(p.209)*: objective, consensus, subjective — strongest to weakest.
- **Three types of model correctness** *(p.211)*: objective, consensus, subjective.

## Fusion Taxonomy (p.211-214) — five classes

| Class | Symbol | Idempotent | Neutral element | Use case |
|-------|--------|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| Belief Constraint Fusion (BCF) | `⊙` | ? | undefined for total conflict | preference intersection (movie pick) |
| Cumulative Belief Fusion (CBF) | `⊕` | NO (non-idempotent) | vacuous | independent evidence accumulation; A-CBF (aleatory) and E-CBF (epistemic) |
| Averaging Belief Fusion (ABF) | `⨁` (boxed plus) | YES | NO neutral element (every opinion adds weight) | dependent sources (jury) |
| Weighted Belief Fusion (WBF) | `⨁̂` (hat) | YES | vacuous | source confidence-weighted (medical experts on multinomial) |
| Consensus & Compromise Fusion (CCF) | `⊚` (circled CC) | YES | vacuous | preserves shared belief, transforms conflict into vague belief; for hyper-opinions |

**Selection procedure** *(Fig. 12.2, p.214)* via four binary questions:
- (b) Should fusing totally conflicting opinions be defined? NO → BCF; YES → continue.
- (d) Should fusing two equal opinions yield equal opinion (idempotence)? NO → CBF; YES → continue.
- (f) Should vacuous opinion be neutral? NO (non-neutrality) → ABF; YES → continue.
- (h) How handle conflicting belief? Weighted average → WBF; Compromise belief (vague) → CCF.

## Operators Defined / Used in This Range (continued)

### Belief Constraint Fusion `⊙` *(Def 12.3, Eq. 12.2, p.216)*

For all `x ∈ R(X)`:

$$b_X^{(A\&B)}(x) = \frac{\mathrm{Har}(x)}{1 - \mathrm{Con}}$$

$$u_X^{(A\&B)} = \frac{u_X^A\,u_X^B}{1 - \mathrm{Con}}$$

$$a_X^{(A\&B)}(x) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{a_X^A(x)(1-u_X^A) + a_X^B(x)(1-u_X^B)}{2 - u_X^A - u_X^B} & \text{if } u_X^A + u_X^B < 2 \\[1ex] \dfrac{a_X^A(x) + a_X^B(x)}{2} & \text{if } u_X^A = u_X^B = 1 \end{cases}$$

with `Har(x)` per Eq. 12.3 and `Con` per Eq. 12.4.

**Properties** *(p.217)*:
- Commutative ✓
- Non-idempotent
- Associative if base rates equal across sources; for unequal base rates, multi-source fusion requires single combined operation
- Neutral element: vacuous opinion `(u^A=1)` *(Eq. 12.5)* — `IF u_X^A = 1 THEN ω_X^A ⊙ ω_X^B = ω_X^B`
- Undefined when `Con = 1` (totally conflicting)
- For unequal base rates, fused base rate is the confidence-weighted average

### Cumulative Belief Fusion `⊕` (aleatory) *(Def 12.5, Eq. 12.14-12.15, p.226)*

**Case I: not both dogmatic** (`u_X^A ≠ 0` or `u_X^B ≠ 0`):

$$b_X^{(A\diamond B)}(x) = \frac{b_X^A(x)\,u_X^B + b_X^B(x)\,u_X^A}{u_X^A + u_X^B - u_X^A u_X^B}$$

$$u_X^{(A\diamond B)} = \frac{u_X^A\,u_X^B}{u_X^A + u_X^B - u_X^A u_X^B}$$

$$a_X^{A\diamond B}(x) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{a_X^A(x)\,u_X^B + a_X^B(x)\,u_X^A - (a_X^A(x)+a_X^B(x))\,u_X^A u_X^B}{u_X^A + u_X^B - 2u_X^A u_X^B} & \text{if } u_X^A\ne 1 \lor u_X^B\ne 1 \\[1ex] \dfrac{a_X^A(x)+a_X^B(x)}{2} & \text{if } u_X^A = u_X^B = 1 \end{cases}$$

**Case II: both dogmatic** (`u_X^A = u_X^B = 0`): use limit weights

$$\gamma_X^A = \lim_{u_X^A,u_X^B\to 0}\frac{u_X^B}{u_X^A + u_X^B},\quad \gamma_X^B = \lim_{u_X^A,u_X^B\to 0}\frac{u_X^A}{u_X^A + u_X^B}$$

$$b_X^{(A\diamond B)}(x) = \gamma_X^A\,b_X^A(x) + \gamma_X^B\,b_X^B(x),\quad u_X^{(A\diamond B)} = 0,\quad a_X^{A\diamond B}(x) = \gamma_X^A\,a_X^A(x) + \gamma_X^B\,a_X^B(x)$$

(Eq. 12.14-12.15, p.226)

## Implementation Notes (continued)
- BCF normaliser `(1 − Con)` ensures additivity of belief and uncertainty masses.
- BCF associativity holds only when base rates are equal; multi-source case with unequal base rates requires single multi-input formula.
- BCF total conflict (Con=1) → undefined output; this is intentional ("preferences are incompatible") not a bug.
- Stochastic-constraint-fusion derivation: relative frequency among non-empty constrained outcomes (denominator excludes ∅ outcomes).
- CBF case II (both dogmatic): the ratio `u_X^B/(u_X^A+u_X^B)` becomes a degenerate weight. Implementations should detect both-zero case and apply Eq. 12.15 with limit-derived `γ` weights — these depend on the relative rate at which uncertainties approach zero, which must be tracked or set by an external rule.

### Aleatory Cumulative Belief Fusion `⊕` — alternate evidence-parameter form *(Theorem 12.2)*

## Implementation Notes (continued)
- **CBF as evidence addition** (Theorem 12.2) is the cleanest implementation path: convert to Dirichlet evidence form, add `r` vectors, convert back. Avoids the case I / case II branching of Def 12.5.
- **ABF non-associativity**: when fusing more than 2 sources, the order matters. For >2 sources use a single multi-input formulation rather than chained pairwise.
- **Aleatory vs epistemic CBF distinction**: aleatory accumulates evidence (uncertainty decreases); epistemic maximises uncertainty post-fusion (uncertainty stays high or grows). Choice depends on whether sources are statistical samples or epistemic witnesses.
- **CBF/ABF dogmatic case II** with `γ=0.5` reduces to simple weighted-belief averaging — the standard fallback.
