# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

Ontologies (Guarino 1998) capture knowledge but fail to capture the structure and use of terms in expressing and referring to this knowledge in natural language. The structure and use of terms is a concern of terminology as well as lexicology. In recent years, the relevance of terminology in knowledge representation has been recognized (again for example the advent of SKOS) and this consideration has been grown at lexical and linguistic issues in knowledge representation (Buitelaar 2010). Natural language is often the medium of choice for knowledge representation and transfer between humans. However, ambiguity is widespread in natural language. Words have multiple meanings and grammar can be ambiguous in structure and therefore in interpretation. However, such ambiguities appear to provide little issue to people, who can with little effort resolve these ambiguities in nearly all situations. Machines, on the other hand, have significant issues in resolving these ambiguities and this can lead to difficulties in defining precise interpretations in technical domains. To illustrate this we will now briefly explain some of the use cases of ontology lexicalization, i.e. knowledge acquisition from text and multilingual knowledge access.

---

## Our Interpretation

The paper addresses the gap between formal ontology knowledge and natural language terminology by proposing the lemon model, an RDF-based lexicon framework that links lexical entries to ontology entities. Its key contribution is a modular, language-independent architecture that separates linguistic knowledge from domain knowledge, enabling multilingual NLP applications over shared ontologies. This is relevant to any system that needs to bridge formal concept representations with natural language surface forms.
