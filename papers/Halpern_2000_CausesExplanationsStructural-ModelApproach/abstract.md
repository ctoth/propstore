# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

We propose a new definition of actual causes, using structural equations to model counterfactuals. We show that the definition yields a plausible and elegant account of causation that handles well examples which have caused problems for other definitions and resolves major difficulties in the traditional account.

---

## Our Interpretation

The paper addresses the long-standing problem that simple counterfactual definitions of causation (X caused Y iff Y would not have happened without X) fail on overdetermination, preemption, and other difficult cases. The key contribution is a three-part formal definition (AC1-AC3) using structural equation models that evaluates counterfactuals under contingencies -- partitioning variables into "frozen" and "free" sets to find conditions under which the cause makes a difference. This is relevant to propstore's argumentation and belief revision layers because it provides a principled formal framework for reasoning about causal relationships between claims, complementing the existing ATMS/AGM infrastructure with a rigorous account of when one event or claim can be said to cause another.
