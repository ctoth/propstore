# Papers To Retrieve

Topic: aspirin for primary prevention of cardiovascular disease in adults without established ASCVD.

Demo question to anchor on:
"Should this patient start or continue low-dose aspirin for primary prevention?"

Why this topic is good for propstore:
- strong real-world relevance
- conflicting trials across eras
- subgroup effects by age, diabetes, and baseline risk
- explicit benefit vs bleeding-harm tradeoff
- guideline reversals after newer evidence

If time is limited, retrieve the papers in Priority 1 first.

## Priority 1: anchor papers for the notebook/demo

1. McNeil JJ, et al. Effect of Aspirin on Disability-free Survival in the Healthy Elderly. N Engl J Med. 2018;379(16):1499-1508.
   Why: cornerstone elderly trial; clean "no net benefit, more bleeding" story.
   Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30221596/

2. McNeil JJ, et al. Effect of Aspirin on All-Cause Mortality in the Healthy Elderly. N Engl J Med. 2018;379(16):1519-1528.
   Why: same ASPREE program; important harm signal and a good example of downstream claim refinement.
   Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30221595/

3. ASCEND Study Collaborative Group; Bowman L, et al. Effects of Aspirin for Primary Prevention in Persons with Diabetes Mellitus. N Engl J Med. 2018;379(16):1529-1539.
   Why: key diabetes subgroup; modest vascular benefit offset by bleeding.
   Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30146931/

4. Gaziano JM, et al. Use of aspirin to reduce risk of initial vascular events in patients at moderate risk of cardiovascular disease (ARRIVE): a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial. Lancet. 2018;392(10152):1036-1046.
   Why: "moderate risk" population; useful for showing that predicted risk and observed event rates are not the same thing.
   Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30158069/

5. Antithrombotic Trialists' (ATT) Collaboration, et al. Aspirin in the primary and secondary prevention of vascular disease: collaborative meta-analysis of individual participant data from randomised trials. Lancet. 2009;373(9678):1849-1860.
   Why: the older pooled evidence base that supported broader aspirin use before the 2018 trials.
   Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19482214/

6. US Preventive Services Task Force. Aspirin Use to Prevent Cardiovascular Disease: US Preventive Services Task Force Recommendation Statement. JAMA. 2022;327(16):1577-1584.
   Why: current US recommendation anchor; converts the trial story into clinical decision thresholds.
   Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35471505/

7. Guirguis-Blake JM, et al. Aspirin Use to Prevent Cardiovascular Disease and Colorectal Cancer: Updated Evidence Report and Systematic Review for the US Preventive Services Task Force. JAMA. 2022;327(16):1585-1597.
   Why: explicit evidence synthesis behind the 2022 USPSTF statement.
   Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35471507/

## Priority 2: older or subgroup-specific trials worth importing

8. Steering Committee of the Physicians' Health Study Research Group. Final report on the aspirin component of the ongoing Physicians' Health Study. N Engl J Med. 1989;321(3):129-135.
   Why: classic positive male-physician primary prevention trial; useful contrast with later evidence.
   Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2664509/

9. Ridker PM, et al. A randomized trial of low-dose aspirin in the primary prevention of cardiovascular disease in women. N Engl J Med. 2005;352(13):1293-1304.
   Why: sex-specific contrast to Physicians' Health Study; stroke signal without MI benefit.
   Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15753114/

10. Ogawa H, et al. Low-dose aspirin for primary prevention of atherosclerotic events in patients with type 2 diabetes: a randomized controlled trial. JAMA. 2008;300(18):2134-2141.
    Why: early diabetes trial; helps show why the field stayed uncertain before ASCEND.
    Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18997198/

11. Belch J, et al. The prevention of progression of arterial disease and diabetes (POPADAD) trial: factorial randomised placebo controlled trial of aspirin and antioxidants in patients with diabetes and asymptomatic peripheral arterial disease. BMJ. 2008;337:a1840.
    Why: another negative diabetes/PAD-flavored primary prevention trial.
    Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18927173/

12. Ikeda Y, et al. Low-dose aspirin for primary prevention of cardiovascular events in Japanese patients 60 years or older with atherosclerotic risk factors: a randomized clinical trial. JAMA. 2014;312(23):2510-2520.
    Why: older higher-risk population before ASPREE; good bridge from pre-2018 to post-2018 thinking.
    Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25401325/

## Priority 3: optional but high-value extensions

13. Zheng SL, Roddick AJ. Association of Aspirin Use for Primary Prevention With Cardiovascular Events and Bleeding Events: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis. JAMA. 2019;321(3):277-287.
    Why: post-2018 synthesis that many clinicians cite when discussing net benefit vs harm.
    Link: https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2721178

14. McNeil JJ, et al. Aspirin, cardiovascular events, and major bleeding in older adults: extended follow-up of the ASPREE trial. 2025.
    Why: likely useful if we want the most recent long-run ASPREE follow-up story, but not required for the first demo pass.
    Link: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40796244/

## What these papers should let us demonstrate

- age-conditioned recommendations
- diabetes as a meaningful subgroup rather than a blanket "high risk" proxy
- evolving evidence over time
- direct benefit-harm conflict between vascular-event reduction and major bleeding
- guideline supersession driven by newer trials and syntheses
