## Observations from pages so far

### Page 0 (cover page)
- Title page from Taylor & Francis / Argument & Computation journal
- Published online 19 Apr 2016

### Page 1 (p.1 of paper)
- Abstract: explains importance of classifying argumentation schemes, outlines how schemes are used in AI
- Keywords: argumentation, argument, interpretation, computational models of argument

### Page 2 (p.2)
- Section 1: Why argumentation schemes are important
- Discusses how the purpose of the classification system will determine the criteria
- Example: classifying animals differently for fur vs observing in wild

### Page 3 (p.3)
- Section 2: Argument mining
- Argumentation schemes with critical matching criteria are proving to be useful for argument mining
- Discusses how schemes can be used to identify and classify instances of argument types
- Mentions legal argument mining, election campaign arguments

### Page 4 (p.4)
- Section 3: Survey of recent classification systems
- Discusses Kienpointner's (1992) taxonomy - three main directions: descriptive/normative distinction, second is the real distinction, schemes vs rules
- Discusses Grennan's taxonomy
- Four categories: quasi-logical, based on reality, establishing reality, and discourse

### Page 5 (p.5)
- Continues survey of classification systems
- Discusses Lumer and Dove (2011) - epistemic vs practical
- Discusses Rahwan, Banerjee, Reed, Walton, and Abdallah (2011) - Argument Interchange Format
- Discusses Bex and Reed (2011) - inference vs conflict schemes
- Discusses Macagno and Walton (2014) - topical relations

### Page 6 (p.6)
- Continues classification discussion
- Bex and Reed (2011) - schemes classified into three broad categories: inference, conflict, preference
- Inference schemes from argument from witness testimony modeled as defeasible modus ponens
- Conflict schemes based on logical conflict by negation or contradiction
- Discussion of how "classification" means grouping, not that some schemes would be classified as others
